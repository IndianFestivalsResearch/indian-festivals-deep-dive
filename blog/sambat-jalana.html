Sambat Jalana: The Festival That Predates Holi

Blog Post #1 — Research Notes — ~4 min read

In 1896, a British civil servant named William Crooke published a two-volume ethnography called The Popular Religion and Folk-lore of Northern India. It was a product of its time — colonial, classifying, occasionally condescending — but it is also one of the most valuable records we have of Indian festival practice before the 20th century.

Buried in Chapter VI of Volume II, after pages of detailed description of Holi bonfires across North India, Crooke mentions something remarkable:

"The Dravidian Hill tribes of Mirzapur do not perform the Holi ceremony like their Hindu neighbours, but on the same date the Baiga burns a stake, a ceremony which is known as Sambat Jalana, or 'the burning of the old year.'"

— W. Crooke, Popular Religion and Folk-lore of Northern India, Vol. II (1896), p. 321

This sentence contains a bombshell for festival history. The Dravidian tribes of the Mirzapur hills — the Baiga, the Gond, the Oraon — were doing something on the Holi date that was not Holi. They were burning the old year. A stake, not a bonfire of collected fuel. A ceremony named after Sambat — the Sanskrit calendar year — but performed by communities largely outside the Sanskritic fold.

What This Suggests

The implications are significant. If the Baiga ceremony is an independent tradition that happens to share a calendar date with Holi, it suggests one of three possibilities:

  1. Holi absorbed a pre-existing year-burning ritual. The Brahminical Holi (with its Holika-Prahlad myth) was layered on top of a much older folk practice of burning the old year — a practice that survived in its "pure" form among communities the Brahminical fold never fully reached.
  2. The calendar date is primordial, the ritual content is variable. The spring equinox period is a universal human festival season. Every culture around the world marks it somehow. The specific forms — bonfires, stakes, colour-throwing — are local adaptations of a shared human impulse.
  3. Sambat Jalana is a borrowing in the other direction. The use of the Sanskrit-derived word "Sambat" suggests possible Hindu influence. But the practice — burning a single stake — is distinct from the Holi bonfire, suggesting independent evolution.

Crooke himself favoured the first interpretation. He concluded that "the fire is really intended to represent the burning of the old year and the re-birth of the new" and that "the Holi, then, in its most primitive form, is possibly an aboriginal usage which has been imported into Brahmanism."

The Significance for Our Project

At Land Of Festivals, we are committed to documenting not just what festivals are but what they were — and what they might have been before the texts were written, before the Brahmins arrived, before the colonial gaze classified everything. Sambat Jalana is a window into that pre-history. It reminds us that every festival is a palimpsest — layers of meaning written over earlier layers, often by communities that left no texts of their own.

We don't know if Sambat Jalana is still practiced. The Mirzapur hills have changed in 130 years. If anyone reading this has seen it — or knows the Baiga communities who might still practice it — please send us a note. This is exactly the kind of living tradition our project exists to document before it disappears.
