Gurpurab

Guru Nanak Jayanti · Prakash Utsav — Celebrating the birth of Guru Nanak Dev Ji and the Kartarpur Corridor's visa-free pilgrimage

Religion: Sikh Key Regions: Punjab, Haryana, Delhi; Kartarpur (Pakistan); global Sikh diaspora Timing: Kartik Purnima (Oct–Nov); the Nanakshahi calendar fixes it on 1 Vaisakh

Current Practices

Gurpurab — literally "the Guru's day" — is the celebration of Guru Nanak Dev Ji's birth anniversary, the most sacred day in the Sikh calendar. The festivities span three days, mirroring the structure established during the Sikh Empire period and maintained globally by Sikh communities today.

Day 1 — Akhand Path: A continuous, uninterrupted reading of the entire Guru Granth Sahib (1,430 pages) begins at the gurdwara. Teams of readers (pathis) read in relays for approximately 48 hours. The Akhand Path is a demanding ritual — maintaining the pace, pronunciation, and melodic recitation without pause requires disciplined scheduling and a pool of trained readers.

Day 2 — Nagar Kirtan: A community procession through the streets, led by the Panj Pyare (Five Beloved Ones) carrying the Sikh flag (Nishan Sahib) and the Guru Granth Sahib on a decorated float. The procession includes kirtan (hymn-singing), martial arts displays (gatka), and community participation. In Punjab and Delhi, Nagar Kirtans for Gurpurab can stretch for kilometres and involve tens of thousands of marchers.

Day 3 — Main Celebration: The actual birth anniversary involves early morning kirtan (Asa di Var), katha (discourse on Guru Nanak's life and teachings), and the culmination of the Akhand Path. The langar serves food continuously throughout the three days, often operating at many times its usual capacity.

The langar at scale: The Golden Temple (Harmandir Sahib) langar at Amritsar serves 50,000–100,000 free meals on a normal day. During Gurpurab, daily service exceeds 300,000 meals — an operation requiring industrial-scale cooking (rotis are made on machines that produce thousands per hour), thousands of volunteers in rotating shifts, and a dishwashing system that handles over 300,000 plates and bowls per day. The langar runs 24/7, 365 days a year, and has never missed a day since its inception.

Guru Nanak (1469–1539): Life and Teachings

Guru Nanak Dev Ji was born in 1469 in Talwandi (now Nankana Sahib, Pakistan) to a Hindu Khatri family. From an early age, he showed an inclination toward spiritual inquiry and a rejection of ritual formalism. At approximately age 30, according to Sikh tradition, he disappeared into the river Bein and emerged three days later with the proclamation: "Na koi Hindu, na koi Mussalman" ("There is no Hindu, there is no Muslim") — not a denial of religious identity per se, but an assertion that true spirituality transcends sectarian labels.

"Truth is higher than everything, but higher still is truthful living." — Guru Nanak, Guru Granth Sahib, Asa di Var

Guru Nanak then embarked on four major journeys (Udasis) spanning over 20 years, covering an estimated 28,000 kilometres on foot — from the Himalayas to Sri Lanka, from Mecca to Baghdad, and as far east as Assam. He preached a radical monotheism, opposition to caste and ritualism, service (seva), honest labour (kirat karo), and sharing with others (vand chakko). His teachings emphasised that the divine could be accessed directly without priestly intermediaries — a theological stance that placed him in tension with both Brahminical Hinduism and orthodox Islam.

Guru Nanak spent the final years of his life in Kartarpur (now in Pakistan), where he established the first Sikh community, practising communal farming and collective worship — a model that evolved into the langar and sangat (congregation) institutions central to Sikhism.

The exact date of Guru Nanak's birth is contested between the historical date (Vaisakh, April 1469, per the Nanakshahi calendar adopted in 2003) and the traditional date (Kartik Purnima, Oct–Nov, per the Bikrami lunar calendar). The Nanakshahi calendar reform has been a source of tension within Sikhism, with some groups, including the Damdami Taksal, rejecting it and continuing to observe the traditional lunar date. The debate reflects deeper divisions within Sikhism about calendrical authority and religious reform.

The Kartarpur Corridor: A 4.7 km Visa-Free Passage

On November 9, 2019, the Kartarpur Corridor was inaugurated — a 4.7-kilometre visa-free passage linking Dera Baba Nanak in Indian Punjab to Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Kartarpur, Pakistan. The corridor allows Indian Sikh pilgrims to visit the site where Guru Nanak spent the last 18 years of his life and where he died in 1539. The gurdwara, located just 4.7 km from the India-Pakistan border, had been visible from Indian territory for decades — a constant, tantalising presence that pilgrims could see but not reach.

The Kartarpur Corridor is a 4.7 km visa-free passage — one of the few peaceful India-Pakistan border crossings, and arguably the only one motivated by religious pilgrimage rather than trade or conflict resolution. The corridor's inauguration in 2019, timed to coincide with Guru Nanak's 550th birth anniversary, was a rare moment of India-Pakistan cooperation. Pakistani Prime Minister Imran Khan and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi both supported the project, though the opening was negotiated separately from broader bilateral tensions.

The corridor's infrastructure is substantial: a passenger terminal building on each side capable of handling 5,000 pilgrims per day, a bridge over the Ravi River, and border security infrastructure on both sides. However, the corridor's operation has been affected by India-Pakistan diplomatic tensions, with pilgrim numbers falling far below capacity during periods of heightened hostility. The corridor exists as a physical manifestation of the gap between the religious and the political — a crossing built for pilgrimage that is subject to the same geopolitical forces that make the India-Pakistan border one of the world's most militarised boundaries.

Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Kartarpur is one of Sikhism's holiest sites — but for over 70 years, it was essentially inaccessible to Indian Sikhs. The original gurdwara was destroyed by floods and rebuilt by the Pakistani government in the 2000s. Before 2019, Indian Sikhs would gather at Dera Baba Nanak on the Indian side and use binoculars to catch a glimpse of the gurdwara's white dome across the border — a ritual of longing that captured the tragedy of Partition's religious geography.

Global Gurpurab Celebrations

Sikh diaspora communities globally observe Gurpurab with rites that closely mirror those in Punjab. In the UK — home to an estimated 432,000 Sikhs — major Nagar Kirtans are held in Southall (London), Birmingham, Leicester, and Glasgow. The Southall Nagar Kirtan draws over 50,000 participants, with streets closed to traffic and the procession passing through one of Britain's most multicultural neighbourhoods.

In Canada — home to the largest Sikh diaspora (approximately 770,000) — Gurpurab parades in Surrey, Brampton, and Mississauga are major civic events. In the United States, the annual Gurpurab celebration at the Sikh Gurdwara of San Jose, California (the largest gurdwara in North America) draws over 20,000 attendees. The diaspora Nagar Kirtan differs from its Punjabi counterpart in significant ways: it is often scheduled for a weekend rather than the precise lunar date (to accommodate work schedules), it includes explicit interfaith outreach, and it serves as a visible assertion of Sikh identity in societies where Sikhs — particularly turbaned men — have faced discrimination and hate crimes in the post-9/11 era.

The Institution of Langar

The langar is perhaps Sikhism's most radical institutional innovation — a practice established by Guru Nanak and institutionalised by Guru Amar Das that enacts the theological principle of equality through the simple act of sitting on the floor and eating together. The langar is vegetarian (to ensure it is accessible to all dietary restrictions), free, and open to all without distinction of caste, religion, gender, or social status.

The Golden Temple's langar is the world's largest free kitchen. The numbers are staggering: on a regular day, 50,000–100,000 meals; during Gurpurab, over 300,000. The operation uses 12,000 kg of wheat flour, 13,000 kg of dal (lentils), and 1,500 kg of rice daily on festival days. The roti-making machines (introduced in 2010) can produce 25,000 rotis per hour. The dishwashing hall processes plates and bowls in a continuous operation. All labour is volunteer (seva), and all ingredients are donated — the langar runs entirely on the Sikh principle of dasvandh (tithing) and community contributions.

The langar at the Golden Temple serves 50,000–100,000 free meals daily under normal conditions. During Gurpurab, this exceeds 300,000 meals in a single day. The kitchen operates entirely on volunteer labour and donations. It has been running continuously since the 16th century — meaning that Sikh langar has survived Mughal persecution, British colonialism, Partition, and the 1984 military operation at the temple itself. Not once in its history has it failed to feed those who came.

Fun Facts

Guru Nanak's travels (the Udasis) covered an estimated 28,000 km on foot — roughly equivalent to walking from London to Beijing and back, or circumnavigating about 70% of the Earth's equatorial circumference. He travelled with a Muslim companion, Bhai Mardana, who played the rabab (a stringed instrument) while Guru Nanak composed and sang hymns — a deliberate interfaith collaboration.

The Guru Granth Sahib contains compositions not only by the Sikh Gurus but also by Hindu and Muslim saints — including Kabir (a Muslim weaver), Namdev (a Hindu tailor), Ravidas (a leather-worker), and Baba Farid (a Sufi saint). This inclusion is unique among world scriptures and enacts Guru Nanak's teaching that divine truth transcends religious boundaries.

Artwork References

Sikh School: Guru Nanak with Bhai Mardana and Bhai Bala (19th century) — Government Museum and Art Gallery, Chandigarh. A classic composition showing Guru Nanak in travelling attire with his two companions, the Muslim rabab-player Mardana and the Hindu devotee Bala — the trio's interfaith composition deliberately emphasised in the iconography.

Guru Nanak Miniatures (18th–19th century) — British Museum, London. The museum holds a collection of miniature paintings depicting scenes from Guru Nanak's life, including the river Bein epiphany, encounters with ascetics, and the Kartarpur community. These paintings, produced in the Pahari and Sikh court traditions, represent the development of a distinctive Sikh visual culture.

Nankana Sahib Frescoes (20th century) — Gurdwara Janam Asthan, Nankana Sahib, Pakistan. The gurdwara marking Guru Nanak's birthplace contains a cycle of frescoes depicting his life, completed during the 1920s–30s Sikh reform movement. The frescoes are in the style of the Sikh school but incorporate elements of the Bengal School revival then influential in Indian art.
