Three Festivals, One Calendar

Critical distinction: Durga Puja, Navaratri, and Dussehra happen on the same calendar dates but are three fundamentally different festivals with different deities, rituals, and emotional registers. Flattening them as "the same festival" is a major research error that this site explicitly avoids.

Current Practices

Durga Puja — Kolkata & Bengal

Kolkata's Durga Puja is a public art spectacle. Over 3,000 community pandals (temporary temples) are erected across the city, many functioning as massive art installations addressing social and environmental themes. The entire city operates on a reversed schedule — pandal-hopping continues past midnight, streets are pedestrianised, and the economy pivots entirely to Pujo. UNESCO inscribed it on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2021.

The artisans of Kumartuli (north Kolkata) create the clay idols in a months-long process. The dhakis — traditional drummers whose rhythm defines the Pujo soundscape — are predominantly Muslim, while the idols depict a Hindu goddess. This inter-dependence is both a testament to Bengal's composite culture and a relationship under strain in the current political climate.

Navaratri — Gujarat's Garba Economy

Gujarat's Navaratri is a nine-night participatory dance festival. Massive garba grounds operate from 9 PM to 3 AM. Ticketed venues, designer chaniya cholis, and NRI tourism have created a substantial seasonal economy. The dance form — circular, communal, with clapping and twirling — is one of the most successfully transmitted festival traditions among diaspora Gujaratis because it's fun (unlike fasting or complex puja rituals).

The Best-Documented Pre-Modern Festival: Vijayanagara Mahanavami (1420–1520)

The Vijayanagara Empire's Mahanavami is the most extensively documented pre-modern Indian festival — with four independent foreign eyewitness accounts. This scale of documentation is unparalleled for any Indian festival before photography.

Abdur Razzak, Persian Ambassador (1443)

"During three consecutive days in the month of Redjeb the vast space of land magnificently decorated... a thousand elephants covered with brilliant armour. Pavilions three, four, or even five storeys high... Some of these pavilions were arranged in such a manner that they could turn rapidly round and present a new face: at each moment a new chamber or a new hall presented itself to the view."

Domingo Paes, Portuguese Traveller (1520)

"These feasts begin on the 12th of September, and they last nine days... On the last day there are slaughtered two hundred and fifty buffaloes and four thousand five hundred sheep... As soon as the sun is down many torches are lit... they begin to throw up many rockets and many different sorts of fires, also castles that burn and fling out from themselves many bombs and rockets."

Paes also describes sixty young women — the queen's ladies — each carrying a golden vessel "as large as a small cask of water" with a lighted lamp inside, processing in order. "So great is the weight of the bracelets and gold and jewels carried by them that many of them cannot support them, and women accompany them assisting them by supporting their arms."

The Mahanavami Dibba (Great Platform) at Hampi — the stone stage where the king sat — still stands. It is an archaeological witness to these accounts.

Fun Facts & Light Mirth

Kolkata's Durga Puja economy is estimated at over ₹50,000 crore — roughly the GDP of a small country. Advertising alone during Pujo exceeds ₹500 crore. The pandal artists of Kolkata are world-class: some have been commissioned for installations at the Venice Biennale.
The rotating pavilions described at Vijayanagara in 1443 were a pre-modern engineering marvel. There is no known parallel in contemporary European or Islamic festival architecture. How they worked — gears? manpower? — remains unclear.
In Mysore, the Dasara procession features a 750-kg golden howdah (elephant seat) carried by the lead elephant. The palace is lit with 100,000 incandescent bulbs — a tradition started in the early 20th century when electricity was still a novelty in India.
The garba dance traditionally moves in a circle around a lit lamp or a goddess image — never the other way. The counter-clockwise motion symbolises the cycle of birth, life, and death. Going clockwise would be... theologically awkward.
Tagore's Santiniketan Basantotsav (spring festival during Dol) involves students and teachers dressed in yellow, singing Rabindra Sangeet, with no colour-throwing at all. It's Holi's polar opposite — a gentrified, intellectualised version that Tagore deliberately created as an alternative to "rowdy" street Holi.

Artwork & References

Company School Paintings of Durga Puja (19th c.) — The British Library and V&A hold paintings commissioned by East India Company officials, depicting household Durga Pujas in Bengal with ethnographic precision — valuable as pre-photography visual evidence of the domestic Puja before the shift to community pandals.
Mysore Dasara Paintings (19th c.) — The V&A and Salar Jung Museum (Hyderabad) hold paintings of the Mysore Dasara procession from the Wadiyar period, showing the elephant procession, palace, and royal ceremonies.
Vijayanagara Archaeological Site, Hampi — The Mahanavami Dibba (Great Platform) is an open-air archaeological site. The relief carvings on the platform depict processions, dancers, musicians, and animals consistent with Paes's 1520 description. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Photography permitted.
Kumartuli Idol-Making Photography — Extensive photojournalism documents the idol-creation process in Kolkata's Kumartuli neighbourhood. A significant visual archive of the material culture behind the festival.

Regional Similarities & Differences

The food ecology of this festival cluster reveals a sharp ritual divide: Bengal's Durga Puja oscillates between satvik vegetarian offerings (bhog) and post-Ashtami meat feasting (mutton, fish). North Indian Navaratri is defined by fasting foods — sabudana, kuttu, singhara — that replace grains with permitted alternatives. Gujarat's Dussehra is defined by jalebi-fafda, a sweet-savoury breakfast entirely distinct from the fasting foods of Navaratri. South Indian Navaratri centres on sundal — a protein-rich legume dish shared communally. The common ritual logic: deity is offered satvik/simple food during worship; the community feasts after.
