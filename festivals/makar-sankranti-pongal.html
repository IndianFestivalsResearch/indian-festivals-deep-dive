Makar Sankranti · Pongal · Lohri · Magh Bihu

Four entirely different festivals sharing a single solar date — the only major Hindu festival on the solar rather than lunar calendar

Religion: Hindu, Sikh, secular (harvest) Key Regions: Tamil Nadu, Punjab, Gujarat, Assam, Bengal, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra, Odisha Timing: January 14 (occasionally 15) — fixed solar date

The Solar Exception

Makar Sankranti is unique among major Hindu festivals: it is the only one fixed by the solar calendar rather than the lunar calendar. The festival marks the sun's transit (sankranti) into Capricorn (Makara), the beginning of Uttarayana — the sun's northward journey — and the end of the inauspicious winter solstice period. Because it is solar, its date is nearly fixed (January 14 or 15), unlike the drifting dates of lunar festivals. This solar anchoring gives Sankranti a pre-Hindu, possibly even pre-Vedic character, linked to ancient solar observation and agricultural cycles.

But "Sankranti" is really an umbrella term for at least four different celebrations — Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Lohri in Punjab, Uttarayan in Gujarat, and Magh Bihu in Assam — that on the same day enact entirely different ritual, culinary, and cultural traditions. They are united not by shared practice but by shared astronomy: the recognition of the sun's pivotal moment in its annual journey.

Why January 14? The sun's entry into Capricorn (Makara Sankranti) occurs when the sun's declination is at 23°26' south and begins moving north. Due to the precession of the equinoxes, the actual astronomical event has drifted from the traditional calendar date — a fact noted by some astronomers but generally ignored by the festival's observers. The date has become a cultural rather than astronomical constant.

Pongal: Tamil Nadu's Four-Day Harvest Festival

Pongal is arguably the most culturally distinct of the Sankranti cluster — a four-day harvest festival that is to Tamil Nadu what Diwali is to North India. It shares no rituals with Lohri or Uttarayan, and its mythological framework is entirely Tamil. The name "Pongal" means "to boil over" in Tamil, referring to the central ritual: the boiling of freshly harvested rice with milk and jaggery in a new earthen pot until it overflows — an act that symbolises abundance and prosperity.

During the Pongal festival, Tamil Nadu consumes an estimated 10,000 tonnes of sugarcane — the entire harvest dedicated to the festival market. Sugarcane stalks are tied at house entrances, given as gifts, and chewed raw by children. This single-crop consumption spike represents one of the most dramatic festival-driven agricultural markets in India.

Each day has a distinct ritual focus: Bhogi (discarding old possessions in bonfires), Surya Pongal (the main day, honouring the sun god), Mattu Pongal (honouring cattle with garlands, painted horns, and special feed), and Kaanum Pongal (family gatherings and community meals). The culinary centrepiece is chakkarai pongal (sweetened rice and moong dal cooked with jaggery, ghee, cashews, and cardamom) and ven pongal (a savoury version with pepper, cumin, and ginger).

Lohri: Punjab's Bonfire Celebration

Lohri, celebrated the night before Makar Sankranti in Punjab and the broader North Indian region, centres on a community bonfire. Families and neighbours gather around a fire fed with wood and cow dung cakes, and offer sesame seeds (til), jaggery (gur), popcorn, and peanuts into the flames while circling the fire singing traditional folk songs. The songs — many of them referencing the legendary folk hero Dulla Bhatti, a 16th-century Robin Hood-like figure who rescued girls from being sold into slavery — give Lohri a distinct cultural texture separate from the purely agricultural Sankranti observances elsewhere.

"Sunder mundriye ho! Tera kaun vichara ho! Dulla Bhatti wala ho!" — Traditional Lohri folk song, referencing Dulla Bhatti's rescue and marriage of two orphaned girls

Lohri is especially significant for families with a newborn child or a newly married couple — their first Lohri is celebrated with particular elaboration, a marker of life-cycle events woven into the agricultural calendar. In Sikh tradition, Lohri additionally commemorates the martyrdom of the Chali Mukte (the Forty Liberated Ones) who died fighting alongside Guru Gobind Singh at the Battle of Muktsar in 1705.

Uttarayan: Gujarat's International Kite Festival

In Gujarat, Makar Sankranti is synonymous with kite-flying — an activity that so dominates the day that the sky over Ahmedabad and other cities becomes a multi-coloured canopy of paper kites. The tradition has been raised to international prominence by the Ahmedabad International Kite Festival, inaugurated in 1989, which now draws kite-makers and flyers from over 40 countries including Japan, Malaysia, USA, UK, and France.

The Ahmedabad International Kite Festival draws participants from over 40 countries. Master kite-makers from Japan fly traditional Rokkaku fighting kites alongside Gujarati patang fighters, while Indonesian and Malaysian flyers bring enormous decorative kites. The festival has become Gujarat's most successful cultural export — more internationally recognised than Navaratri garba.

The competitive aspect is crucial: Gujarati kite-flying involves pench ladana (kite fighting), where flyers coat their strings with a mixture of fine glass powder and glue (manjha) to cut opponents' lines. The cry of "Kai Po Che!" ("I have cut it!") echoes across rooftops. This practice has drawn criticism for causing injuries to birds and humans, and some cities have imposed time restrictions on manjha use.

The festival's culinary signature is undhiyu (a mixed vegetable dish slow-cooked in an earthen pot) and chikki (brittle made from sesame seeds and jaggery), the latter linked to the Sankranti tradition of consuming til-gur (sesame-jaggery) as a warming food at winter's peak.

Magh Bihu: Assam's Feast of Fire

In Assam, Makar Sankranti is celebrated as Magh Bihu (or Bhogali Bihu) — the harvest festival of the Assamese calendar. On the eve of Magh Bihu (Uruka), communities build temporary shelters (bhela ghar) out of thatch and bamboo, where they feast communally through the night on freshly harvested rice, fish, and meat. At dawn, the bhela ghars are set ablaze in a ritual fire, and the day is spent in community sports (buffalo fights, egg fights, tekeli bhonga — blindfolded pot-breaking).

The Assamese Magh Bihu feast (bhogali bhoj) is distinctive: dishes include laru (sesame and rice flour sweets), pitha (rice cakes stuffed with sesame and coconut), and roasted fish and meat cooked over open fires. The festival has strong elements of pre-Hindu animist and tribal practices overlaid with Vaishnava Hindu elements introduced during Sankardev's 15th–16th century neo-Vaishnavite movement.

Historical Origins

The solar festival of the winter solstice / sun's northward turn is ancient across Indo-European cultures. In the Vedic period, Uttarayana was considered an auspicious period, and the Shatapatha Brahmana (c. 8th–6th century BCE) describes the sun's northward course as the path of the gods. The specific festival of Makar Sankranti appears in the Mahabharata: Bhishma, lying on his bed of arrows after the Kurukshetra war, waits for Uttarayana to begin before choosing to die, as death during the sun's southward journey (Dakshinayana) was considered less auspicious for liberation.

"Bhishma, the son of Shantanu, having been pierced by arrows, lay on a bed of arrows, waiting for the sun to turn northward." — Mahabharata, Anushasana Parva, abridged

The Iranian festival of Mehregan (honouring Mithra, the sun deity) and the Korean Jeongwol Daeboreum (Great Full Moon festival) share uncanny similarities with Sankranti traditions — midwinter timing, bonfires, sesame-and-nut-based foods, and rituals of discarding the old. These parallels may reflect a shared prehistoric Indo-European solar-festival substratum stretching from Iran to East Asia, or independent convergences around agricultural and solar cycles.

Regional Similarities & Differences

The culinary logic of this festival cluster is seasonal warming foods. Sesame (til) and jaggery (gur) appear across all regional forms — from Maharashtra's tilgul to Punjab's rewri to Karnataka's ellu-bella — because they are traditional winter-warming foods in Ayurveda. The festival marks the sun's northward turn and the end of winter; the food is designed to generate body heat. Pongal in Tamil Nadu is both a dish and a festival — the only Indian festival named after food. The rice-and-dal combination in Pongal is structurally similar to khichdi across North India — both are one-pot rice-lentil dishes — but Pongal has a specific sweet/savoury festival identity that khichdi lacks. Undhiyu is unique to Gujarat and represents the winter vegetable harvest; there is no direct parallel in other regions' Sankranti foods.

Attire — Clothing & Accessories 👗

Sankranti/Pongal attire is defined by region-specific traditional garments and the colour black — uniquely for this festival, black clothing is considered auspicious in Maharashtra and parts of North India during Sankranti (winter warmth symbolism). Pongal in Tamil Nadu is the silk-saree-and-veshti occasion of the year. Lohri in Punjab features bright winter colours.

Evolution Through Time

Pre-colonial Pongal attire was defined by handloom cotton and early silk-weaving traditions. Kanjeevaram silk weaving received royal patronage from the Chola and Vijayanagara empires. The modern Pongal silk-shopping season is a post-Independence commercial evolution. The "black on Sankranti" tradition in Maharashtra appears in 19th-century records — its antiquity is uncertain but the winter-warmth rationale is consistent with pre-modern clothing logic. The Gujarati kite-flying festival's rooftop gatherings shaped attire pragmatically: comfortable, colourful, and visible from neighbouring rooftops.

Regional Similarities & Differences

Same calendar date, four different clothing traditions. Tamil Pongal: heavy silk, gold temple jewelry, flowers in hair — the most formal. Punjabi Lohri: bright wool and phulkari — winter warmth meets festival colour. Gujarati Uttarayan: bandhani sarees and comfortable kite-flying clothes. Maharashtrian Sankranti: black clothing (unique among Indian festivals) and til-gul exchange attire. The common thread across all regions: seasonal appropriateness — these are winter harvest festivals and the clothing reflects January temperatures (silk and wool in the North; silk and cotton in the South).
