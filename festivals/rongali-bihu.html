Rongali Bihu

The Assamese New Year & Spring Festival
🌾 Assamese (pan-community) 📍 State festival of Assam 📅 April 13–15 (coincides with Vaisakhi solar date) 🐄 Goru Bihu (cattle) · Manuh Bihu (people) · Gosai Bihu (gods)

Three Bihus, One Assam

Assam has three Bihu festivals, not one. They mark the agricultural calendar: Rongali Bihu (or Bohag Bihu, April — spring sowing, the main festival), Kongali Bihu (or Kati Bihu, October — lean period, austerity), and Bhogali Bihu (or Magh Bihu, January — harvest feast, covered in our Makar Sankranti page). Rongali Bihu is by far the most important — it's the Assamese New Year and a week-long celebration that has resisted Sanskritisation more successfully than almost any other major Indian festival.

Not just a harvest festival: Unlike Magh Bihu (which is explicitly about food and abundance), Rongali Bihu is about renewal, fertility, love, and community. The Bihu songs are often secular, romantic, and sometimes bawdy — a folk tradition that predates Hinduisation.

The Days of Rongali Bihu

DayNameRitual
Day 1Goru BihuCattle are bathed, their horns oiled, hooves cleaned, garlanded, and fed special treats. Cows are the centre of attention — a throwback to the agrarian roots. In the evening, cattle are herded back with fresh ropes.
Day 2Manuh Bihu"Human Bihu." New clothes, elders are honoured with a ceremonial gamosa (traditional Assamese towel). Family feasting. Young people seek blessings from elders.
Day 3Gosai Bihu"God's Bihu." Temple visits, prayers for a good harvest. Household deities are worshipped.

Bihu Dance & Music

The Bihu dance — performed by young men and women in groups — is characterised by brisk, rhythmic hip movements, hand gestures, and rapid footwork. Women wear the mekhela chador (traditional Assamese silk), while men wear dhoti-kurta with a gamosa tied around the head. The music is driven by the dhol (drum), pepa (buffalo-horn pipe), gagana (bamboo jaw harp), and toka (bamboo clapper).

The Bihu dance was once considered "indecent" by 19th-century Assamese Hindu reformers (influenced by Victorian morality via British colonialism), who tried to suppress it. It survived because it was too deeply embedded in village life. Today, competitive Bihu dance is televised, and the "Bihu idol" format draws massive viewership in Assam.

Fun Facts

Bihu songs are secular love poetry. Unlike most Indian festival music (devotional bhajans, kirtans), Bihu geet are about romantic love, longing, nature, and everyday village life. The pepa (buffalo-horn flute) has a distinctive reedy sound that is synonymous with Bihu for every Assamese person. Bihu album releases are a major music-industry event in Assam, with sales rivalling Bollywood within the state.
The gamosa — a white cotton towel with red woven borders — is the universal symbol of Assamese identity. During Bihu, it is offered to elders as a sign of respect, tied around the head during dance, and exchanged between friends. It is so culturally significant that it has a GI (Geographical Indication) tag.
Rongali Bihu coincides with 13 other Indian New Year festivals: Baisakhi (Punjab), Vishu (Kerala), Puthandu (Tamil Nadu), Poila Boishakh (Bengal), and others. Across India on the same April days, entirely different festivals are unfolding — a testament to the subcontinent's diversity.

Regional Similarities & Differences

Bihu food is agrarian Assamese cuisine at its most celebratory. Unlike Sankranti/Pongal (same solar date — entirely different food), Bihu embraces meat, fish, and rice beer alongside rice-flour sweets. The pitha tradition is shared across Eastern India — Bengal's pithe, Odisha's pitha, Bihar's thekua — but Assamese pitha leans more on rice flour and jaggery than wheat, reflecting the geography. Rice beer at Bihu parallels the ritual fermented drinks of Northeast indigenous festivals (Wangala, Chapchar Kut) and Himalayan festivals (Losar's chang) — a pan-Himalayan/NE fermented-grain tradition.
