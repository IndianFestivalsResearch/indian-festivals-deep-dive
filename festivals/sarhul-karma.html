Sarhul & Karma

The Adivasi spring and autumn festivals — Sal flower worship, the marriage of Earth and Sun, and the demand for Sarna religious recognition in the Indian census

Tradition: Adivasi (Sarna / Indigenous — Munda, Oraon, Ho, Santhal, Kharia) Key Regions: Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, West Bengal, Assam, Madhya Pradesh Timing: Sarhul (Mar–Apr, Chaitra); Karma (Aug–Sep, Bhadrapada)

Adivasi Festivals, Not Hindu Festivals

Sarhul and Karma are among the most significant festivals of the Adivasi (tribal/indigenous) peoples of the Chotanagpur plateau and surrounding regions — primarily the Munda, Oraon, Ho, Santhal, and Kharia communities. These are not Hindu festivals, though they are often miscategorised as such in census records, government documents, and popular media. They belong to Sarna — the indigenous religion of the Adivasi peoples, centred on the worship of nature, ancestors, and village deities, with the Sal tree (Shorea robusta) as its most sacred symbol.

The distinction between Sarna and Hindu practice is politically charged. For decades, Adivasi activists have demanded a separate Sarna religious code in the Indian census, arguing that clubbing Adivasis under "Hindu" erases their distinct religious identity and facilitates the Hinduisation (and consequent cultural assimilation) of their communities. The demand has gained momentum: in 2021, the Jharkhand legislative assembly passed a resolution requesting the central government to include a Sarna code in the 2021 census. As of 2026, the demand remains unmet, but the movement has transformed festival documentation — recording Sarhul and Karma not as "tribal curiosities" but as expressions of an autonomous religious tradition — into a political act.

The Sarna religious code movement is a demand by Adivasi communities to be counted separately from Hindus in the Indian census. Currently, Adivasis who practise Sarna (nature/ancestor worship centred on the Sal tree) are classified as "Hindu" or "Other" in the census. Activists argue this is a form of statistical erasure that facilitates Hinduisation and land dispossession — if Adivasis are counted as Hindus, their distinct legal and constitutional protections as Scheduled Tribes can be subtly undermined. The demand for a Sarna code makes festival documentation — recording Sarhul and Karma as Sarna, not Hindu, festivals — a political act of identity assertion.

Sarhul: The Spring Festival of Sal Flowers

Sarhul (from sar = year, hul = beginning — "the beginning of the year") is the spring festival celebrated in Chaitra (March–April), coinciding with the flowering of the Sal tree. It is simultaneously a New Year, a harvest festival, and a ritual of nature worship — Sarna theology does not separate these categories as Western or Hindu thought might. The Sal tree is the axis mundi of Sarna cosmology: the Sarna Sthal (sacred grove) in every Adivasi village contains Sal trees that are believed to be the dwelling place of the village deities and ancestral spirits.

The festival is inaugurated by the Pahan (village priest, always from the original founding clan of the village), who performs rituals at the Sarna Sthal. Three new earthen pots filled with water are placed beneath the Sal tree; if the water level drops over the following days, it is taken as an omen of a good monsoon. The Pahan offers Sal flowers, handia (fermented rice beer), and the sacrifice of a chicken (the blood is sprinkled on the Sal tree's roots), followed by a communal feast. The distribution of Sal flowers — which every household receives and places in their home shrine, granary, and agricultural implements — is the festival's central ritual act.

Sal trees are considered so sacred in Sarna religion that during Sarhul, even the leaves are not plucked — they are collected only after they naturally fall. The Sal flower (Sarhul phool) is believed to carry the blessing of the earth deity and the ancestors. After the Pahan distributes the flowers, they are placed not only in homes but in fields, granaries, wells, and on farming tools — a ritual that sacralises the entire agricultural cycle. The Sal tree is so central to Sarna identity that Adivasi activists sometimes describe their religion as "the religion of the Sal forest" (Sarhul dharam).

The day after the ritual is marked by communal dancing and singing — the Jadur, Mardani Jhumar, and Domkach dance forms, performed in circles to the rhythm of the mandar (drum), nagara (kettle drum), and bansi (flute). The dances are not mere entertainment — they are forms of communal prayer, with specific dance patterns, musical structures, and song lyrics that encode Sarna theology, seasonal knowledge, and social values. Men and women dance together in most Sarna traditions, a practice that distinguishes Sarna festivals from many Hindu and Muslim observances where mixed-gender dancing is restricted.

Karma: The Autumn Festival of the Karma Tree

Karma (or Karam) is celebrated in Bhadrapada (August–September) and is associated with the Karam tree (Adina cordifolia, or sometimes the Kadamba tree). While Sarhul celebrates the agricultural New Year and the Sal tree, Karma focuses on the Karam tree as a symbol of prosperity, fertility, and the well-being of brothers. The festival is particularly associated with the Oraon, Munda, and Kharia communities of Jharkhand, and has spread with Adivasi migration to the tea gardens of Assam and West Bengal.

The ritual structure mirrors Sarhul: a branch of the Karam tree is brought to the village dancing ground (akhra), planted, and worshipped by the village priest. Women play a central role — they fast through the day (the Karam fast, like many Adivasi fasts, is observed by women for their brothers' well-being, and some scholars see a structural parallel with Raksha Bandhan), circumambulate the Karam branch, and lead the community in song and dance through the night. The songs narrate the Karam legend — the story of seven brothers, their neglect of the Karam tree, and the misfortunes that befell them until they restored its worship — a moral tale encoding the Sarna principle of reciprocity between humans and nature.

Handia (rice beer) is ritually essential to both Sarhul and Karma — it is offered to the deities, consumed by the participants, and plays a role in ritual toasting and community bonding. The centrality of handia has periodically brought Adivasi communities into conflict with state prohibition laws. Several Indian states have attempted to ban or restrict the production and consumption of handia under liquor prohibition, leading to protests by Adivasi groups who argue that handia is a ritual substance, not a commercial intoxicant, and that its prohibition is a violation of religious freedom.

Historical Origins

The origins of Sarhul and Karma are lost in prehistory — they are the festivals of a forest-dwelling people whose oral traditions stretch back millennia, well before the arrival of Indo-Aryan speakers in the Gangetic plain. Archaeological evidence of continuous settlement in the Chotanagpur plateau dates to the Mesolithic period (c. 10,000–5,000 BCE), and linguistically, the Munda languages (including Mundari, Santali, and Ho) belong to the Austroasiatic language family, distinct from the Indo-European and Dravidian languages that dominate India.

The earliest written references to what may be Sarhul and Karma appear in medieval Hindu and Islamic texts, though these are filtered through the perspective of external observers who often described Adivasi practices as "primitive," "animist," or "demonic." The British colonial period produced more systematic ethnography — W.G. Archer's studies of Munda and Oraon culture, S.C. Roy's monumental The Mundas and Their Country (1912), and Verrier Elwin's work on Adivasi art and ritual — but these too were shaped by colonial categories. A genuinely self-representational Adivasi literature on Sarhul and Karma has emerged only in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, through the work of Adivasi scholars, activists, and writers publishing in Mundari, Kurukh, Santali, Hindi, and English.

"The Mundas know no distinction between sacred and secular in the modern sense. Their festivals are simultaneously religious observances, agricultural ceremonies marking the rhythm of the seasons, and social gatherings that cement the bonds of the community." — S.C. Roy, The Mundas and Their Country, 1912

Festivals as Political Identity

In the 21st century, Sarhul and Karma have moved from rural, village-level observances to urban, politically conscious public festivals. This transformation parallels the trajectory of other regional Indian festivals (Chhath in Delhi, Pongal as a Tamil identity marker) but carries a sharper political edge. In Ranchi, Jharkhand's capital, the state government now organises an official Sarhul celebration — a recognition of the festival's political weight in a state where Adivasis form approximately 26% of the population and are a decisive electoral constituency.

The urban Sarhul is a hybrid: traditional rituals are performed by a Pahan, but the event also includes speeches by political leaders, cultural programmes featuring Adivasi musicians and dancers, and public distribution of handia (where legal). The festival's visual iconography — the Sal flower, the red-and-white Sarna flag — has become a symbol of Adivasi identity deployed in political campaigns, land rights movements, and protests against mining and displacement. The demand for a Sarna census code — which Sarhul celebrations now explicitly foreground — has become the single-issue rallying point uniting diverse Adivasi communities across linguistic and regional differences.

The demand for a separate Sarna religious code is opposed by some Hindu nationalist organisations who argue that it represents an attempt to divide Hindu society and that Adivasi religions are essentially "folk Hinduism." This framing is rejected by Adivasi activists, who point out that Sarna theology — with its emphasis on nature worship, absence of a creator deity, absence of Vedic texts or Brahmin priests, and distinct cosmology — is fundamentally different from Hinduism and deserves recognition as an independent religious tradition, not a subset of it.

Fun Facts

The word "Sarna" refers both to the sacred grove at the centre of every Adivasi village and to the religious tradition itself. The Sarna Sthal is a protected area of original forest where the Sal trees stand, and where no tree may be cut, no animal hunted, and no crops planted — making it one of the oldest forms of community-managed conservation in the world. In an era of global deforestation, the Sarna Sthal — thousands of them across Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh — represent living seed banks of native Sal forest biodiversity protected by religious sanction.

The Adivasi dance forms performed during Sarhul and Karma — Jadur, Mardani Jhumar, Domkach, Chhau (partially Adivasi-influenced) — are almost always circular and involve the entire community, young and old. The circle is not an aesthetic choice but a theological one: it represents the equality of all participants, the cyclical nature of the seasons, and the indivisibility of the community. The mandar drum, made from a hollowed tree trunk with leather stretched over both ends, is the heartbeat of the dance — its rhythms encoding the specific footwork patterns that dancers learn from childhood.

Many Adivasi communities observe a sarhul bandi (Sarhul holiday) that can last up to a week, during which no agricultural work is done. The land is believed to be "menstruating" or "resting" during this period — a concept that encodes both ritual observance and sound agricultural practice (the post-harvest spring is the right time to let fields lie fallow and regenerate). The idea of the earth as a living, gendered body that requires periods of rest is central to Sarna cosmology.

Artwork References

Adivasi Folk Art and Sarna Symbols — Indira Gandhi Rashtriya Manav Sangrahalaya (IGRMS), Bhopal. This museum holds one of the largest collections of Adivasi material culture in India, including ritual objects from Sarhul and Karma celebrations — decorated handia pots, musical instruments (mandar, nagara, bansi), and photographs of sacred groves. The collection documents the material culture of Sarna religion, which is primarily expressed through objects, spaces, and performances rather than texts.

Sohrai and Khovar Wall Paintings — Jharkhand, practised by Munda, Oraon, and Santhal women. While Sohrai (harvest) and Khovar (marriage) are distinct from Sarhul and Karma, they belong to the same Sarna cultural complex. These mural traditions — in which women paint the mud walls of their homes with geometric and animal motifs using natural pigments — represent one of India's oldest continuous art traditions. The paintings, like the festivals, encode seasonal knowledge and Sarna cosmology.

Colonial-Era Ethnographic Photography (early 20th century) — British Museum / British Library, London. Photographs by colonial administrators and anthropologists (W.G. Archer, Verrier Elwin, J.H. Hutton) documenting Munda, Oraon, and Santhal festivals. These photographs, while created within a colonial ethnographic framework, are now being recontextualised by Adivasi scholars as visual records of traditions that have since changed or been lost.

Food & Diet — Festival Table 🍽️

Sarhul and Karma are Adivasi (tribal) spring and monsoon harvest festivals of the Jharkhand-Chhattisgarh-Odisha-West Bengal belt, primarily celebrated by the Munda, Oraon, Ho, and Santal communities. The food is characterised by its direct, unmediated relationship to the land: rice from the field, greens and roots foraged from the forest, meat (chicken, goat, sometimes pig) from the village, and the ritually essential rice beer (handia) fermented at home. Everything is local, seasonal, and minimally processed. The food is cooked communally by the village and shared equally among all — the act of communal eating IS the act of worship. Sal flowers (Sarhul) and the Karam tree branch (Karma) are ritually central but are not eaten.

Regional Similarities & Differences

Among the Munda community, Sarhul food includes handia (rice beer), ara (sacrificial meat, typically chicken or goat), sweet rice, and foraged greens — the meal follows a specific ritual sequence: handia first offered to the earth, then meat, then rice. The Oraon community's Karma festival features specific foods offered to the Karam tree branch — often a more vegetarian-leaning offering in some villages, with milk and fruits alongside the standard rice and greens. Santal communities in Jharkhand and West Bengal prepare pitha (rice cakes) during Sohrai-Sarhul and have a stronger tradition of foraging wild mushrooms from the sal forest — a seasonal delicacy. The Ho community of Singhbhum features dishes incorporating mahua flowers and mahua liquor alongside handia, with a characteristic preference for boiling over frying. Urban and Christian-converted Adivasi communities may substitute meat with vegetarian options and omit handia, though the communal meal structure remains intact. The common food philosophy across all Adivasi festivals is: the food is gathered, not bought; cooked together, not individually; shared equally, not portioned by social status. This distinguishes Adivasi festival food sharply from the grain-temple-offering model of Hindu festivals and the charity-meal model of Sikh and Muslim celebrations.

Attire — Clothing & Accessories 👗

Sarhul and Karma festival attire is handwoven, community-specific, and deeply tied to forest, land, and ancestral identity. Among Munda, Oraon, Ho, and Santal communities, each has distinct textile traditions woven on backstrap or pit looms. During Sarhul (spring festival), the sal flower (Shorea robusta) worn in hair and behind ears is THE defining festival accessory — no other Indian festival uses a specific wildflower as the primary sartorial element. Women wear handwoven sarees with tribe-specific borders and patterns; men wear dhoti or lungi with ganji (vest) or simple kurta. Karma festival features specific dance attire for the Karam tree ritual: community-specific colours and patterns, with women circling the Karam branch in coordinated traditional dress. Adivasi festival attire emphasises community textile traditions over individual adornment — the aesthetic is collective, practical, and inseparable from the land and forest that sustain the community.

Evolution Through Time

Adivasi textile traditions predate the Aryan-Vedic arrival in the subcontinent by millennia — the backstrap loom is among the oldest weaving technologies, and community-specific weave patterns have been transmitted through women for countless generations. The sal flower as Sarhul accessory is a direct remnant of pre-Vedic forest worship — the flower of the sal tree, sacred to the Munda and Oraon as the abode of the village deity, worn as bodily adornment during the spring ritual. Colonial-era forest laws (Indian Forest Act 1865, 1878) disrupted the relationship between Adivasi communities and their forests, but the sal flower tradition persisted. Christian missionary activity in the 19th–20th centuries led some converted Adivasis to abandon traditional attire and sal-flower wearing as "pagan" — but post-independence Adivasi cultural revival movements have reclaimed Sarhul and Karma as pan-tribal identity festivals, bringing traditional dress back to public prominence. The Sohrai-Khovar mural and textile art of the Hazaribagh region has received national and international recognition (UNESCO intangible heritage consideration), linking festival attire to a broader textile-art tradition.

Regional Similarities & Differences

Among the Munda community, Sarhul is the premier festival — women wear the lugda (handwoven cotton saree) with specific red-and-white border patterns, and the sal flower is worn ubiquitously by all ages. Oraon men and women traditionally wear white cotton with minimal jewellery; their Karma dance attire features coordinated colours for group formations. Ho community women of Singhbhum (Jharkhand) wear distinctive handwoven sarees with broader borders and prefer heavier silver jewellery compared to other Adivasi groups. Santal communities in Jharkhand and West Bengal celebrate Sohrai (related harvest festival) with pitha-making and have distinct textile traditions — Santal women's sarees often feature specific checks and stripes woven on the loom. In Chhattisgarh, the Gond and Baiga communities have their own versions of Karma with distinctive attire — Baiga women traditionally wore minimal clothing (pre-colonial), now adapted to contemporary cotton sarees and kurtis. Urban Adivasi communities (Ranchi, Jamshedpur, Rourkela) increasingly wear factory-made garments for daily life but return to handwoven traditional attire for Sarhul and Karma — the festival functions as a sartorial homecoming. The sal flower as the defining accessory, however, remains unique to Sarhul — no other Indian festival designates a wildflower as its primary sartorial element.
