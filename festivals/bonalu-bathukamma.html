Bonalu & Bathukamma

Telangana's twin state festivals — Bonalu's goddess pots and tranced dancers, Bathukamma's women-led floral cone-stacks — elevated as identity markers after 2014 statehood

Religion: Hindu (Shakta, folk) Key Region: Telangana (particularly Hyderabad and Telangana districts) Timing: Bonalu (Ashada month, Jul–Aug); Bathukamma (Bhadrapada, Sep–Oct, nine days culminating in Durgashtami)

Two Festivals, One Identity

Bonalu and Bathukamma are Telangana's defining festivals — two separate observances that together form a seasonal pair, the former occurring during Ashada (the monsoon month, July–August) and the latter during Bhadrapada (September–October). Both are folk Shakta festivals centred on the goddess, both are women-led (though in different ways), and both were elevated from regional celebrations to state-level festivals following the formation of Telangana as India's 29th state in 2014. The post-statehood promotion of Bonalu and Bathukamma as "Telangana's festivals" is a case study in how political identity-making can transform religious festivals.

Before 2014, both festivals were well-known within Telangana but nearly invisible to the rest of India. The Telangana Rashtra Samithi (now Bharat Rashtra Samithi) government, led by K. Chandrashekar Rao, made the promotion of Telangana's cultural distinctiveness — including Bonalu and Bathukamma — a key element of state-building. Government funding for festival celebrations increased dramatically; official events were organised at the state, district, and mandal levels; and the festivals were promoted as tourism attractions. The result has been a dramatic increase in visibility and scale — but also, some argue, in state co-option and folklorisation.

Bonalu: The Plague-Averting Goddess Festival

Bonalu (from bhojanalu, "food offerings") is a month-long Shakta folk festival celebrated in the Telangana region, with its epicentre in Hyderabad and Secunderabad. The festival involves devotees — overwhelmingly women — offering bonam: a decorated earthen pot filled with cooked rice, jaggery, and curd, topped with a clay lamp and neem leaves, carried on the head to the goddess temple. The pot itself is a work of folk art — painted with turmeric, vermillion, and white rice-flour designs — and the procession of hundreds of women carrying these decorated pots through the streets is the festival's visual signature.

The goddess honoured during Bonalu is the village mother goddess — Mahankali, Yellamma, Pochamma, Maisamma — in her various local manifestations. The festival proceeds through the city in a specific sequence: the Golconda Mahankali temple, the Secunderabad Ujjaini Mahankali temple, and the Old City's Lal Darwaza temple each have their designated Bonalu Sundays, creating a rolling festival that occupies the entire month of Ashada. At each temple, the bonam pots are offered, animal sacrifices are performed (chickens and goats, though this practice is declining), and Pothurajus — men dressed as the goddess's brother, their bodies painted with turmeric, wearing red dhotis and ankle bells, and carrying whips — dance in a state of trance possession.

The Bonalu festival was originally a plague-averting ritual. According to local tradition, in 1813, a cholera outbreak in Hyderabad was ravaging the population. A military battalion from Secunderabad, stationed at the Ujjaini Mahankali temple area, prayed to the goddess and vowed to offer Bonalu if the plague abated. When the outbreak subsided, the battalion organised the first Bonalu, and the tradition has continued for over 200 years. Historical records confirm a major cholera epidemic in Hyderabad around this period. The festival thus has a documented origin in a specific public health crisis, giving it a historical specificity unusual for Indian folk festivals.

The Pothuraju dance is Bonalu's most dramatic element. Pothuraju is believed to be the brother of the goddess, who accompanies her during the festival. The dancers enter trance states, lash themselves with their whips (in some cases drawing blood), smear turmeric on their bodies, and dance barefoot on hot coals or broken glass. The tranced Pothuraju is believed to be possessed by the goddess's brother and to act as her guardian during the procession. The dance — wild, percussive, and physically extreme — has no counterpart in the more sedate Brahminical temple rituals of mainstream Hinduism, and represents a survival of pre-Brahminical, possibly pre-Aryan possession cults within Telangana's folk religion.

Trance possession and its acceptability: The Pothuraju dance, with its self-flagellation and fire-walking, has drawn attention from rationalist and reformist groups who criticise it as dangerous or superstitious. Defenders counter that trance possession is a legitimate religious experience that should not be judged by standards derived from textual, priest-mediated religious traditions. The debate is part of a larger conversation about folk religion, bodily practice, and the limits of religious tolerance in modern India.

Bathukamma: The Festival of Life

Bathukamma — literally "Mother, come alive" (bathuku = life, amma = mother) — is a nine-day floral festival celebrated during Bhadrapada (September–October), culminating on Durgashtami, two days before Dussehra. Unlike Bonalu's fiery intensity, Bathukamma is a festival of colour, community, and women's creativity — a celebration of life, fertility, and the seasonal abundance of Telangana's monsoon flora.

The central ritual is the creation of Bathukammalu — cone-shaped or pyramidal stacks of seasonal wildflowers arranged in concentric rings on a wide brass or copper plate (thambalam). The flowers — gunugu (Celosia), tangedu (Cassia), banti (marigold), chamanthi (chrysanthemum), gummadi puvvu (pumpkin flower), and at least a dozen other varieties — are arranged by women in community groups, working together through the afternoon. The stack grows through the nine days of the festival: on day one, a simple, small arrangement; by day nine, an elaborate floral tower that can reach six feet in diameter and four feet in height.

Bathukamma involves creating floral towers that can reach 6 feet in diameter using seasonal wildflowers — essentially a massive floral engineering project undertaken by women across Telangana. The construction requires knowledge of flower varieties, colour theory, structural stability (the cone must be self-supporting), and community coordination. A full-sized Bathukamma can use over 50 varieties of flowers and take a team of 5–10 women 3–4 hours to build. The result is a temporary artwork — displayed, danced around, and then immersed in water at the end of the day, making the entire process impermanent, like the Tibetan sand mandala.

In the evening, women gather in circles around their Bathukammalu, singing folk songs (Bathukamma paatalu) and performing a distinctive dance: stepping in and out of the circle in synchronised patterns, clapping hands, and moving in unison. The songs cover a wide thematic range — the beauty of nature, the pain of separation from loved ones, the joys of sisterhood, social satire, and direct addresses to the goddess Gauri (Parvati). After the singing, the Bathukamma is carried in procession to the nearest water body and immersed (nimajjanam) — a ritual that parallels the Ganesh visarjan and Durga visarjan but occurs on a community-women scale rather than a public-crowd scale.

The final day — Saddula Bathukamma — is the grand culmination, involving the largest and most elaborate Bathukamma, a community feast, and the largest immersion procession. The following day, male relatives are served a special meal prepared by the women — a structural inversion of the male-headed feast typical of most Hindu festivals.

After Telangana statehood in 2014, the state government organised a Bathukamma with over 10,000 women participating simultaneously at Hyderabad's LB Stadium — one of the largest coordinated female ritual performances ever staged. The event was part of a deliberate strategy to elevate Bathukamma as a symbol of Telangana identity on par with major Indian festivals. The government also funded Bathukamma celebrations in all 32 districts and promoted the festival internationally through Telangana diaspora associations in the US, UK, and Gulf countries.

Post-Statehood Elevation: A Political Festival-Making

The promotion of Bonalu and Bathukamma as state festivals after Telangana's formation in 2014 is one of the most deliberate cases of political festival-making in contemporary India. The Telangana government allocated significant funding — the 2022 state budget allocated ₹235 crore for Bathukamma celebrations alone, including ₹10,000 per woman in self-help groups for purchasing flowers and sarees. The government declared public holidays for both festivals, organised official state-level events, and deployed tourism marketing to brand the festivals as Telangana's cultural calling card.

This elevation has had complex effects. On one hand, it has brought unprecedented visibility and resources to festivals that were previously confined to Telangana's villages and urban neighbourhoods. Women who make Bathukammalu have gained recognition as folk artists; the floral economy has received a major boost (an entire seasonal flower supply chain has emerged to meet Bathukamma demand); and the festivals have become a source of pride and identity for Telanganites, both in the state and in the diaspora.

Critics argue that state sponsorship has folklorised and commercialised Bonalu and Bathukamma — transforming them from organic community celebrations into government-managed events with political speeches, corporate sponsorship, and tourism marketing. The ₹235 crore allocation for Bathukamma, while popular with women voters, has been criticised as an electoral bribe dressed as cultural promotion. Defenders argue that all major Indian festivals — Diwali, Durga Puja, Ganeshotsav — have been shaped by political and commercial forces, and that Telangana's festivals deserve the same state support that festivals in other states receive.

Historical Origins

The origins of both Bonalu and Bathukamma lie in pre-Brahminical folk religion. Bonalu is clearly a Shakta mother-goddess festival with roots in the village goddess (grama devata) traditions that predate the Sanskritisation of Telangana's religious landscape. The animal sacrifice, trance possession, and non-Brahmin priesthood (pujaris are typically from non-Brahmin castes) mark it as a folk rather than textual tradition. The plague-averting origin story (the 1813 cholera outbreak) is unusually specific, but it likely represents a particular historical moment when an older folk ritual was institutionalised and monumentalised.

Bathukamma's origins are similarly folk and pre-Brahminical. The name itself is a Telugu folk construction, not a Sanskrit-derived term. The centrality of wild seasonal flowers — rather than cultivated temple flowers — and the all-female performance structure suggest origins in women's agricultural and fertility rituals that were later framed as a goddess festival. Some scholars see Bathukamma as a surviving form of an ancient matrilineal women's ritual, possibly dating to the megalithic cultures of the Deccan (c. 1000 BCE–300 CE).

"The women's songs of Bathukamma contain a world — the pain of the daughter-in-law, the longing for the mother's home, the beauty of the Telangana landscape — that has no place in the Sanskritic textual tradition. These songs are a women's literature, transmitted orally for centuries, largely unknown to men and to the literate world." — Jayadhir Thirumala Rao, Telangana Folk Culture, 1998

Gender and Festival Structure

Both Bonalu and Bathukamma are structurally centred on women, but in different ways. Bonalu's primary ritual actor is the woman who carries the bonam pot on her head to the temple — a physically demanding act that requires strength, balance, and endurance (the pots are heavy, and the processions can last hours in the July heat). The Pothurajus are men, but they are supporting actors; the goddess's primary devotees and the festival's organisers are women.

Bathukamma is almost entirely female-led and female-performed. Men's role is largely limited to logistical support — collecting flowers, preparing food for the feast, and childcare during the women's afternoon-long gathering. Within the women's circle, there is no priest, no hierarchy, and no formal ritual expert — every woman is a participant, and the knowledge of flower arrangement and song lyrics is shared and transmitted horizontally. This radically egalitarian structure — unusual in Indian religious practice, which is typically hierarchically organised around male priests or ritual specialists — may represent a survival of pre-patriarchal women's ritual culture.

The songs themselves merit attention as a women's oral literature. Bathukamma songs address the goddess Gauri (Parvati) as sister, mother, and friend — not as the remote cosmic power of Sanskritic theology but as an intimate presence in the everyday lives of women. The songs also contain extensive social commentary: satires of oppressive mothers-in-law, laments of daughters married into distant villages, celebrations of female friendship, and jokes at the expense of men. This body of folk literature — oral, female, in Telugu vernacular, and largely unrecorded — represents a cultural archive that is transmitted exclusively through festival participation.

Fun Facts

The flowers used in Bathukamma are all locally grown, seasonal wildflowers — not commercially cultivated varieties. The festival thus functions as a spontaneous celebration of Telangana's monsoon biodiversity. Botanists have identified over 80 distinct flower species used across different districts of Telangana for Bathukamma, many of them medicinal plants (gunugu, tangedu, lotuses). The festival inadvertently serves as a conservation mechanism: communities maintain wildflower habitats to ensure a steady supply for the annual celebration.

The Bonalu festival of the Ujjaini Mahankali temple in Secunderabad has a distinctive ritual: a male devotee dressed as a matangi (female form of the goddess, associated with an "untouchable" caste incarnation) parades through the streets throwing turmeric and vermillion powder at onlookers. This ritual — which involves a male devotee embodying a female, Dalit-associated form of the goddess — is a striking example of the gender and caste fluidity that folk religion permits but orthodox Hinduism typically suppresses.

The Bonalu festival's iconic food item is sarvapindi — a savoury rice-flour pancake studded with chana dal, peanuts, chillies, and curry leaves, cooked on a clay griddle. It is offered to the goddess and consumed by devotees. The dish is specific to Telangana and has no analogue in neighbouring Andhra cuisine — a culinary marker of the distinctiveness that the Telangana movement has been at pains to emphasise.

Artwork References

Telangana Folk Art: Bonalu Procession (contemporary) — Salar Jung Museum, Hyderabad. The museum holds a collection of paintings and photographs documenting Bonalu celebrations in Hyderabad, including representations of the Pothuraju dancers and bonam-bearing women. These works document a folk festival within a museum context that traditionally privileged courtly and colonial art.

Bathukamma Photographic Documentation (2014–present) — Telangana Tourism Department archives. Following statehood, the tourism department commissioned extensive photographic documentation of Bathukamma celebrations across all districts, creating a visual archive of the floral stacks, women's circles, and immersion processions that had previously received minimal institutional attention.

Deccani Miniature: Mahankali Temple Scene (18th century) — Various collections. Deccani miniature paintings occasionally depict the Mahankali temples of the Golconda/Hyderabad region, providing visual evidence of the goddess cults that form the substrate of Bonalu practice, predating the 1813 plague story.
