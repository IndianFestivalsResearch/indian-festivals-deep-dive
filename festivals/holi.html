Holi

The Spring Festival of Colours
🎨 Hindu · Cultural · Sufi 📍 Northern & Western India (minor in South) 📅 February–March (Phalguna Purnima) ⏳ Earliest literary reference: Harsha Charitra (7th c. CE); likely far older

How It Is Celebrated Now (2020s)

Holi is a festival of licensed transgression. "Bura na mano, Holi hai" — Don't mind, it's Holi — is the cultural permission slip for behaviour that would be unacceptable any other day. Strangers throw coloured powder at each other. Water balloons and pichkaris (water guns) drench passers-by. Bhang (cannabis-infused drink) is widely consumed, particularly in North India.

The epicentre remains Braj (Mathura, Vrindavan, Barsana, Nandgaon in Uttar Pradesh), where Holi extends for weeks. The Lathmar Holi of Barsana — where women ritually beat men with sticks — draws international tourists and Instagram influencers. The Widows' Holi in Vrindavan (begun around 2012–13) broke a centuries-old taboo: widows, traditionally excluded from colour and celebration as inauspicious, now play Holi publicly — a deeply symbolic act covered by global media.

Urban India has split Holi into two tiers: the traditional street Holi (messy, public, class-mixing) and the ticketed "Holi party" (DJ, organic/herbal colours, rain dance setups, bhang counters). The chemical-versus-herbal colour debate is significant — cheap synthetic powders containing oxidised metals cause skin and eye damage.

Holi is also the festival where the phrase "Holi is not a license to molest" became a necessary public campaign, addressing the reality that the festival's transgressive licence is exploited for sexual harassment in some urban contexts.

The great regional divide: Holi is a minor festival across most of South India. Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, and Andhra have limited Holi observance. Where it exists, it's driven by North Indian migration or specific community traditions. The Kamdev narrative (Shiva burns the god of love) exists in Tamil tradition but doesn't drive mass colour-play. On the same full-moon day, Manipur celebrates Yaosang (6-day festival with Thabal Chongba dance), Goa celebrates Shigmo (street processions with floats), and Kumaon celebrates Khadi Holi (white clothes, classical ragas — barely any colour).

Four Competing Origin Stories — Documented by Crooke (1896)

The British ethnographer W. Crooke documented four different origin stories for Holi during his fieldwork in the 1890s — and concluded that the real origin was probably none of them:

"One account of its origin describes it as founded in honour of a female demon or Rakshasi called Dundhas, 'she who would destroy many.' Another account connects the observance with the well-known legend of Hiranya-kasipu and his son Prahlada... A third legend identifies Holi with the witch Putana, who attempted to destroy the infant Krishna by giving him her poisoned nipple to suck. Lastly, a tale told at Hardwar brings us probably nearer the real origin of the rite. Holika or Holi was, they say, sister of Sambat or Sanvat, the Hindu year. Once, at the beginning of all things, Sambat died, and Holi in her excessive love for her brother insisted on being burnt on his pyre, and by her devotion he was restored to life. The Holi fire is now burnt every year to commemorate this tragedy."

— W. Crooke, Popular Religion and Folk-lore of Northern India, Vol. II (1896), pp. 313–314

Crooke's own interpretation — and that of subsequent scholars including D.D. Kosambi — is that the Holi bonfire is primarily a sun charm: "magical ceremonies intended to ensure a proper supply of sunshine for men, animals, and plants." The Holi fire represents the burning of the old year. The Dravidian Hill tribes of Mirzapur, Crooke noted, do not perform Holi but on the same date burn a stake in a ceremony called "Sambat Jalana" — the burning of the old year.

The Vishnu Purana (Book I, Chapter XX) tells the Prahlad story and notes that reading it on full moon or new moon days "shall yield fruit equal to the donation of a cow" — but does NOT explicitly connect it to Holi. The explicit Prahlad-Holika-Holi link is a later synthesis.

What Vijayanagara Eyewitnesses Saw (1420–1520)

Two remarkable accounts capture pre-modern Holi practice:

"There are also three other festival days, during which they sprinkle all passers-by, even the king and queen themselves, with saffron water, placed for that purpose by the wayside. This is received by all with much laughter."

— Nicolo Conti (Italian merchant), c.1420, Vijayanagar

This account from 600 years ago shows that the basic Holi gesture — public colour-play involving everyone from kings to commoners — is remarkably stable. However, Conti describes saffron water, not dry powder. The transition from liquid to powder (gulal/abir) appears to have occurred later, perhaps during the Mughal period when refined pigments became more available.

The Mughal court celebrated Holi extravagantly. Amir Khusrau (13th century) wrote Holi verses. Jahangir's memoirs describe court Holi. Bahadur Shah Zafar, the last Mughal emperor, composed Holi poetry. This Sufi Holi tradition — "Eid-e-Gulabi" (the Pink Eid) in Persian sources — is increasingly erased in contemporary political discourse that frames Holi as exclusively Hindu.

Fun Facts & Things of Note

The Sanskrit playwright Banabhatta described a spring festival with coloured powders in the 7th century CE — making Holi one of the oldest continuously celebrated festivals with literary documentation. But it might be even older: the 4th–5th century Puranic references suggest pre-existing traditions.
At the Lathmar Holi in Barsana, women wield actual sticks (lathis) and men protect themselves with shields. It's been happening for centuries and, remarkably, major injuries are rare. The men who get hit consider it a blessing. The women consider it extremely satisfying.
The Bhojpuri folk song tradition of Chowtal — sung during Holi in Bihar and Eastern UP — is now better preserved in Trinidad than in India. Songs that have faded in their region of origin survive in the Caribbean indenture diaspora, creating a remarkable cultural time capsule.
In 2023, an estimated 50,000 litres of bhang were legally consumed in Varanasi alone during Holi — and that's before counting home-brewed supplies. The government-licensed bhang shops in Varanasi have existed since the British Raj.
The "Holi Festival of Colors" brand now runs events in 40+ countries. Some are organised by non-Indian companies. This has sparked a complex debate: cultural diffusion versus cultural appropriation. The truth, as always, is messier than either position.

Artwork & Visual References

Mughal Holi Miniatures (16th–18th c.) — The British Museum and Chester Beatty Library hold Mughal paintings showing court Holi celebrations. Women spraying coloured water on each other and the emperor; nobles in white clothes stained pink. Search: "Mughal Holi painting." These are the most detailed pre-photographic records of Holi practice.
Kangra/Pahari Holi Paintings (18th c.) — The V&A and National Museum, New Delhi, hold Pahari paintings of Krishna playing Holi with Radha and the gopis — the romantic-devotional Holi imagery that defines the festival's aesthetic.
Barsana Holi Photography (20th–21st c.) — Contemporary photojournalism of Lathmar Holi has produced an extensive visual archive. Magnum Photos and National Geographic have documented Braj Holi extensively. These are copyrighted but viewable online.
Jahangirnama Paintings (17th c.) — The Mughal emperor Jahangir's memoirs include painted scenes of Holi in the court. These show the cross-community character of Mughal Holi — Muslim nobles participating alongside Hindus.

Regional Similarities & Differences

The Holi table unites North and West India's sweet-fried repertoire with the cooling fermented and dairy-based drinks that counter the spring heat. Gujiya at Holi vs. Gujiya at Diwali: same dumpling, different festival meaning — at Holi it's associated with Krishna's childhood pranks; at Diwali with Lakshmi's abundance. In Maharashtra, Puran Poli replaces gujiya as the festival flatbread — a shift from wheat-crust pastry to lentil-stuffed whole-wheat bread. Thandai and bhang connect Holi to Shiva's association with cannabis in Shaivite tradition — though the bhang-Holi link is a folk practice, not a scriptural mandate. The consumption of fermented foods (kanji) at Holi parallels spring fermentation traditions worldwide — a practical pre-refrigeration preservation technique.

Attire — Clothing & Accessories 👗

Holi attire is defined by one rule: wear something you're willing to ruin. Unlike Diwali's new-silk-and-gold aesthetic, Holi clothes are old, white (for showing the colours), and disposable. The white kurta stained pink, green, and yellow is the most iconic Holi image. In some traditions (Kumaoni Khadi Holi, evening visits), fresh white clothes are worn precisely to showcase the transformation.

Garment styles, fabrics, and accessorising traditions vary significantly by region, community, class, and generation. Diaspora celebrations may adapt traditional attire to local climates and contexts. Traditional handloom textiles and artisanal jewelry are living craft traditions — not costumes.

Evolution Through Time

Pre-colonial Holi dress was likely everyday village clothing — Holi was a festival of the fields, not of finery. The colonial period brought synthetic gulal (replacing natural turmeric/sandalwood powders) which stains more aggressively — making the "old white clothes" practice essential rather than optional. Bollywood Holi sequences (from "Rang Barse" onwards) created the visual archetype: white clothes, coloured powder, wet fabric clinging dramatically. Post-2000s: designer "Holi collection" clothing — pre-dyed, colour-splattered patterns that mimic Holi stains without the mess. The commercialization of Holi fashion contradicts the festival's fundamental anti-fashion ethos.

Regional Similarities & Differences

White cotton is the Holi uniform across North and Central India — the logic is universal: start clean, end coloured. But regional forms radically diverge: Kumaoni Khadi Holi (white, formal, classical music) is the polar opposite of Braj street Holi (white, disposable, chaos). Dol Jatra in Bengal is more about devotional attire (yellow, the colour of spring and Krishna) than disposable white. Yaosang in Manipur involves traditional dance costumes — the garment logic is performance, not destruction. Goan Shigmo involves elaborate carnival-style costumes and floats. The common thread: Holi attire is situationally appropriate — the "rule" is determined by which Holi you're attending.
