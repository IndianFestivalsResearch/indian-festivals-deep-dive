Diwali · Deepavali

The Festival of Lights
🪔 Hindu · Jain · Sikh 📍 Pan-Indian (except Kerala) 📅 October–November (Kartika Amavasya) ⏳ Documented since at least 4th c. BCE (Jain); debated older origins

How It Is Celebrated Now (2020s)

Diwali is India's most commercially significant festival. The "Diwali shopping season" rivals Christmas in the West — Amazon and Flipkart run their largest sales. Gold purchases spike; new clothes are mandatory; homes are repainted; diyas (oil lamps) are lit in every window.

The five-day structure in North India runs: Dhanteras (wealth, metal purchases), Narak Chaturdashi / Chhoti Diwali (pre-dawn oil bath in the South), Lakshmi Puja / main Diwali (goddess of wealth), Govardhan Puja / Annakut (mountain of food), and Bhai Dooj (sibling bond). In Bengal, the same night is Kali Puja — a completely different deity.

The firecracker debate has become a defining modern tension. Supreme Court restrictions on cracker timing and types, Delhi NCR's hazardous air quality, and campaigns for "Green Diwali" by schools and NGOs coexist with a right-wing framing of cracker bans as "anti-Hindu." The child-labour firecracker industry in Sivakasi, Tamil Nadu, is a well-documented but under-enforced crisis.

Digital Diwali is now a layer: WhatsApp aarti forwards, Zoom family pujas (normalised by COVID-19), virtual rangoli competitions, and e-diya apps. Corporate Diwali gifting — branded dry-fruit boxes — has become a ritual obligation of its own.

South India note: In Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Andhra, Diwali is celebrated in the morning — pre-dawn oil bath, new clothes, crackers at sunrise. Naraka Chaturdashi is the climax, not Lakshmi Puja. This is a fundamentally different festival texture from the North Indian evening celebration.
Firecracker timeline: We lack solid evidence that firecrackers were a mass consumer Diwali item before the 19th century. Gunpowder was known in India, but the firecracker-as-Diwali-tradition likely entered via Chinese trade routes in the 19th–20th centuries. The "ancient tradition" claim lacks primary-source support.

Seven Competing Origin Stories

Diwali is unique in having not one but at least seven origin narratives, held by different communities — and they coexist rather than conflict in lived practice. However, political Hindu nationalism increasingly foregrounds the Rama-Ayodhya narrative to the exclusion of others.

What the Earliest Sources Say

The Jain Account — 4th Century BCE

"In that night in which the Venerable Ascetic Mahâvîra died... the eighteen confederate kings of Kâsî and Kosala, the nine Mallakis and nine Likkhavis, on the day of new moon, instituted an illumination on the Poshadha, which was a fasting day; for they said: 'Since the light of intelligence is gone, let us make an illumination of material matter!'"

— Kalpasutra, Life of Mahavira, Lecture 5, §128 (tr. Hermann Jacobi, 1884)

The Ramayana Account — Rama's Return

"Thus borne along in royal state / King Rama reached Ayodhya's gate / With merry noise of shells and drums / And joyful shouts, He comes, he comes... Neath gorgeous flags that waved in rows / On towers and roofs and porticoes. / Mid merry crowds who sang and cheered / The palace of the king they neared."

— Ramayana, Book VI, Canto CXXX (tr. Ralph T.H. Griffith, 1874)

The Colonial Account — 1896

"The original basis of the feast seems to have been the idea that on this night the spirits of the dead revisit their homes, which are cleaned and lighted for their reception. Now it is chiefly observed in honour of Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth and good luck, who is propitiated by gambling. On this night the women make what is called 'the new moon lampblack' (Amawas ka Kajal), which is used throughout the following year as a charm against the Evil Eye."

— W. Crooke, Popular Religion and Folk-lore of Northern India, Vol. II (1896), pp. 295–297

Diaspora: From Indenture Ships to the White House

Diwali is the most visible Indian festival globally. The White House has held an annual Diwali celebration since the George W. Bush administration. 10 Downing Street, Trafalgar Square, and Times Square host major Diwali events. New York City schools began closing for Diwali in 2023.

In the Caribbean (Trinidad, Guyana, Suriname), the descendants of indentured labourers have preserved Diwali as a national holiday. Trinidad's Divali Nagar is a week-long festival. Bhojpuri folk traditions — including Chowtal songs — survive in the Caribbean in forms that have faded in Bihar itself.

In Malaysia and Singapore, Deepavali is a public holiday with the distinctive "Open House" tradition — non-Indians are welcomed into Indian homes, a reversal of the domestic privacy that characterises Indian celebrations.

The Gulf labour diaspora — millions of Indian workers in the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar — celebrate Diwali in labour camps and shared flats. This is the largest and least-documented Diwali diaspora.

Light Mirth & Things of Note

During Diwali, Indians buy more gold than the entire gold reserves of many small countries — over 20 tonnes in a single day of Dhanteras shopping.
The Ayodhya Deepotsav in 2023 lit over 2.2 million diyas simultaneously along the Sarayu river — setting a Guinness World Record. The electricity bill alone was... substantial.
In Nepal, the second day of Tihar is Kukur Tihar — dogs are garlanded, given treats, and honoured as messengers of Yama (god of death). Even stray dogs get the VIP treatment. In 2016, Nepal Police dogs participated in an official ceremony with tilak and garlands.
The gambling tradition during Diwali has a mythological justification: Parvati played dice with Shiva and won. Gambling on Diwali night is considered auspicious — though most people lose, which is less auspicious.
The word "Diwali" comes from the Sanskrit Deepavali — "row of lamps." In Tamil, it's Deepavali; in Nepali, Tihar; in Bengali Kali Puja areas, they don't call it Diwali at all. The pan-Indian "Diwali" brand erases these differences.

Artwork & Visual References

Mughal Diwali Miniatures (16th–18th c.) — The British Museum holds several Mughal paintings showing court Diwali celebrations with lamps, fireworks, and gift exchanges. Search "Mughal Diwali" at britishmuseum.org/collection. Many images available under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0.
Pahari Paintings of Rama's Return (18th c.) — The V&A Museum has Pahari (Kangra/Guler) paintings depicting Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana returning to Ayodhya with Hanuman. These are among the finest visualisations of the Diwali origin story.
Kalpasutra Manuscript Paintings (15th c.) — The Chester Beatty Library (Dublin) holds Jain Kalpasutra manuscripts with painted folios showing Mahavira's nirvana — lamp-lit scenes that visualise the earliest documented Diwali origin. Digital collection accessible online.
Raja Ravi Varma's Lakshmi (1890s) — The iconic image of Goddess Lakshmi standing on a lotus, widely reproduced as Diwali posters, originates from Ravi Varma's oleographs. The original paintings are in private collections and museums.
Company School Paintings (19th c.) — The British Library holds Company School paintings commissioned by East India Company officials, depicting Indian festivals including Diwali with ethnographic detail — valuable visual records of pre-photography practice.

Modern Tensions & Debates

  • Air pollution: Delhi NCR's post-Diwali AQI regularly exceeds 400 (hazardous). Supreme Court mandated "green crackers" and time restrictions — compliance is mixed.
  • Child labour: Sivakasi, Tamil Nadu, produces ~90% of India's firecrackers. Child labour in the industry is well-documented, with periodic government raids and persistent under-enforcement.
  • Widow exclusion: In some communities, widows are considered inauspicious and excluded from Lakshmi Puja. The Vrindavan widows' Diwali celebration (since ~2013) is a powerful counter-narrative.
  • Consumer debt: Diwali spending creates financial pressure on lower-income households. The "obligation to spend" is a documented stress factor.
  • Political Hinduism: The Rama-Ayodhya narrative is increasingly centred to the exclusion of Jain, Sikh, and folk origins — part of a broader majoritarian reframing of Indian festivals.

Regional Similarities & Differences

Diwali food maps to region, not uniformly to Diwali. Gujiya/Shankarpali dominate in the North and West; Murukku/Seedai in the South; Anarsa/Chivda in Maharashtra; mutton curry in Bengal (Kali Puja — same calendar date, different festival). The common thread is ghee, sugar, and deep-frying — the trinity of Indian festival indulgence. Gujiya and Karanji are structurally the same dumpling (stuffed, sealed, fried) but the filling differs by region: khoya in the North, coconut in the West and South. Motichoor Laddoo and Besan Laddoo share the fundamental technique of binding fried gram flour with sugar syrup but produce entirely different textures.
