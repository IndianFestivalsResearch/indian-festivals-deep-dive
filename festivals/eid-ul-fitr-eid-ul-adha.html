Eid-ul-Fitr · Eid-ul-Adha

Islamic Festivals in the Indian Subcontinent
☪️ Muslim (all traditions) 📍 Pan-Indian (wherever Muslim communities live) 📅 Eid-ul-Fitr: end of Ramadan (1 Shawwal) · Eid-ul-Adha: 10 Dhu al-Hijjah ⏳ Celebrated in India for ~1,400 years (since 7th century CE)

How Eid Is Celebrated Now (2020s)

Eid-ul-Fitr — The Sweet Eid (Meethi Eid)

Eid-ul-Fitr marks the end of Ramadan, the month of dawn-to-dusk fasting. The day begins with the Eidgah congregation prayer — massive open-air gatherings in parks, maidans, and the grounds of historic mosques. The Delhi Jama Masjid and Hyderabad's Mir Alam Idgah draw hundreds of thousands. The prayer is followed by the ritual embrace and greeting: "Eid Mubarak."

The defining food is sewaiyan / sheer khorma — sweet vermicelli cooked in milk with dates, nuts, and saffron. Every Muslim household in India, from Kashmir to Kerala, produces some version of it. Eidi — money given by elders to children — has undergone significant inflation: what was ₹10–50 in the 1990s is now ₹500–2,000+ in middle-class families.

Eid-ul-Adha — The Feast of Sacrifice (Bakri Eid)

Eid-ul-Adha commemorates Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son. The ritual of Qurbani (animal sacrifice) divides the meat into three portions: one-third for the family, one-third for relatives and friends, one-third for the poor.

Political reality: In India, cow sacrifice (religiously permitted but legally restricted by state-level cow protection laws) has made Qurbani a site of communal tension and violence. Most Indian Muslims now sacrifice goats, sheep, or buffaloes instead. The transport of animals for Qurbani, visible blood in public spaces, and disposal of remains generate practical and political conflicts every year. This is NOT a theological issue — it's a political one.

What Ibn Battuta Saw — Eid at the Delhi Sultanate (1334)

The 14th-century Moroccan traveller Ibn Battuta left the most detailed pre-modern description of Eid celebrations in India — at the court of Muhammad bin Tughluq in Delhi:

"On the eve of the festival the Sultan sends robes of honour to the maliks, the courtiers, the chief officers of state... On the morning of the feast day all the elephants are adorned with silk, gold, and precious stones. There are sixteen of these elephants... over them are carried sixteen parasols of silk embroidered with jewels, each one with a shaft of pure gold... The great throne is set up at the upper end of the hall; it is of pure gold throughout, and its legs are encrusted with jewels. Its length is twenty-three spans... The great cassolette, which is a sort of tower of pure gold... Inside it are three chambers which are entered by incense-burners, who set fire to aloes-wood, ambergris and benzoin."

— Ibn Battuta, Rihla (1325–1354), Delhi section

The Sultan's Eid celebrations lasted seven days: Day 1 — feast and audience; Day 2 — musicians and dancers; Day 3 — marriages of royal relatives; Day 4 — emancipation of male slaves; Day 5 — emancipation of female slaves; Day 6 — marriages between slaves; Day 7 — distribution of alms.

Origin in India: The Cheraman Juma Masjid in Kodungallur, Kerala, claims to be India's first mosque, built c. 629 CE — within the lifetime of the Prophet Muhammad. If the dating is accepted, Eid has been celebrated in India for nearly 1,400 years. However, some historians dispute this dating. The earliest structural evidence is later. Present the claim with the archaeological caveat.

Fun Facts & Things of Note

The moon-sighting controversy is an annual feature of Indian Eid. Different committees (Shahi Imam, local mosque committees, Markazi Chand Committee) sometimes announce Eid on different days, leading to the uniquely Indian phenomenon of "moon fighting" — spirited WhatsApp debates, memes, and the eternal question: "Did you see the moon or not?"
In Hyderabad, the Ramzan month transforms the old city into an all-night gastronomic festival. Haleem — a slow-cooked wheat, meat, and lentil stew — is so central that the city consumes an estimated ₹100 crore worth of haleem during Ramadan. It has a Geographical Indication (GI) tag.
The Bohra community (Dawoodi Bohra) uses a calculated calendar rather than moon-sighting, so they always know exactly when Eid will be — no moon-fighting for them. Their distinctive communal meal (thaal) involves 8–10 people eating from a single large platter, an egalitarian practice called thaal communion.
Eid in Kashmir includes the tradition of visiting graves of family members and Sufi saints (ziyarat) — a practice that some reform movements consider un-Islamic but remains deeply embedded in Kashmiri Muslim culture.
The Khoja Ismaili community in Gujarat celebrates Eid with dandiya raas (garba) — a merging of Gujarati Hindu and Ismaili Muslim cultural forms. It's one of the most striking examples of syncretic festival practice in India. The practice is entirely orthodox within their tradition.

Diaspora — From Trafalgar Square to the Gulf

In the UK, Eid in Trafalgar Square draws over 20,000 people. Leicester, Birmingham, and London host massive community events blending Indian, Pakistani, and Bangladeshi Eid traditions. Some UK school districts now recognise Eid as a holiday.

In the USA, "Eid festivals" have adopted the American suburban format — held at Six Flags, amusement parks, and convention centres. "Eid-gifting" has increasingly borrowed from the Christmas gift format (wrapped presents, gift trees), creating a hybrid practice that some embrace and others resist.

The Gulf labour diaspora — millions of Indian Muslim workers in the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar — celebrates Eid in severely constrained conditions: labour camps, shared flats, limited time off. This is the largest and least-documented Eid population. Workers who can afford to return home for Eid do so; those who cannot celebrate modestly, often separated from their families for years.

Artwork & References

Mughal Eid Miniatures (16th–19th c.) — The British Museum and Chester Beatty Library hold Mughal paintings depicting Eid processions and court ceremonies. Search "Mughal Eid painting." The Ain-i-Akbari describes Akbar's Eid celebrations in detail.
Jama Masjid Photography (19th–21st c.) — The Delhi Jama Masjid's Eid congregation has been extensively photographed since the 19th century. Felice Beato (1850s) and subsequent photographers have created a visual archive of one of India's largest annual gatherings.
Hyderabad's Old City — Ramzan Night Markets — Contemporary photography and documentary work extensively covers the Charminar area during Ramadan. The illuminated Charminar and the food markets around it are among the most photographed festival spaces in India.
