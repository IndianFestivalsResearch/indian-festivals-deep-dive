Maha Shivaratri

The Great Night of Shiva · Herath · Mandi Shivaratri — All-night vigil, fasting, and the convergence of 200+ village deities

Religion: Hindu (Shaiva) Key Regions: Pan-Indian; especially Varanasi, Ujjain, Mandi (HP), Kashmir, Tamil Nadu, Nepal Timing: Phalguna Krishna Chaturdashi (Feb–Mar)

Current Practices

Maha Shivaratri is unique among Hindu festivals in being a night festival par excellence — the "Great Night of Shiva." The core practice is jagaran: staying awake through the night in vigil, typically at a Shiva temple or in a domestic shrine. Devotees observe a strict fast (some abstain even from water), and perform abhishek (ritual bathing) of the Shiva lingam with water, milk, honey, curd, ghee, and bilva (bael) leaves, each substance carrying symbolic significance. The Shiva lingam is bathed every three hours (prahara) through the night.

At the twelve Jyotirlinga temples — sites where Shiva is believed to have manifested as a pillar of infinite light — the scale is immense. The Kashi Vishwanath temple in Varanasi, the Somnath temple in Gujarat, and the Mahakaleshwar temple in Ujjain each draw over a million devotees on this single night. The Mahakaleshwar temple's Bhasma Aarti — the pre-dawn ritual in which the lingam is adorned with fresh cremation-ground ash — is performed with particular grandeur on Shivaratri, and the temple remains open continuously for 24 hours.

In Tamil Nadu, devotees walk barefoot to major Shiva temples — some covering over 100 kilometres over several days — in a practice called pada yatra. At the Annamalaiyar temple in Tiruvannamalai, devotees circumambulate the sacred Arunachala hill (14 km) on foot through the night.

Historical Origins

The origins of Shivaratri observances are ancient. The Skanda Purana and Linga Purana (both c. 7th–10th centuries CE) describe the ritual of night-long worship of Shiva on the fourteenth day of the dark half of Phalguna. The story told is that of a hunter who, unknowingly fasting and staying awake in a bilva tree, dropped its leaves on a Shiva lingam below, and was thus granted liberation — an origin myth that democratises the festival by making its first devotee an illiterate tribal hunter.

"On the fourteenth day of the dark half of the month of Phalguna, the worship of Shiva at midnight confers great merit. One should stay awake through the night, offering bilva leaves, and the fruits of one's fast are multiplied a thousandfold." — Skanda Purana, Brahmottara Khanda

The festival also commemorates the divine marriage of Shiva and Parvati in some traditions, and the night when Shiva performed the Tandava — the cosmic dance of creation, preservation, and destruction — in others. This multiplicity of meanings is characteristic of Shivaratri, which functions more as a cluster of related observances than a single, unified festival.

Archaeological evidence of Shiva worship — in the form of lingam stones and seals depicting a horned, cross-legged figure identified by some scholars as proto-Shiva — dates to the Indus Valley Civilisation (c. 2500–1900 BCE), suggesting that Shaiva traditions have deep prehistoric roots, though the specific festival of Shivaratri cannot be traced that far back with certainty.

The Indus Valley "Pashupati seal" showing a horned figure surrounded by animals is widely cited as evidence of proto-Shaivism. Some scholars argue this is a speculative reading and that the figure may represent a generic shamanic or animal-master deity unrelated to the historical Shiva.

Mandi Shivaratri: The Parliament of Gods

The most extraordinary regional observance of Shivaratri occurs not at a major temple city but in the small Himalayan town of Mandi in Himachal Pradesh. The Mandi Shivaratri mela — a week-long event — involves over 200 village deities (gram devtas) being carried down from their mountain shrines in decorated palanquins by devotees to pay homage to Madho Rai (a form of Vishnu/Shiva) and the presiding deity Bhootnath (a form of Shiva) in Mandi town.

At Mandi Shivaratri, over 200 village deities — each represented by a mohra (metal face-mask) carried in a decorated palanquin — converge on the town. This gathering is called the "Parliament of Gods" because the deities are believed to have their own hierarchies, territories, and political relationships that are reaffirmed through the precise order of procession and seating within the fairgrounds. Some deities travel for days, carried on foot by devotees from villages at 3,000 metres altitude.

The origins of the Mandi fair date to 1526 CE, when Raja Ajbar Sen of Mandi invited all the valley's deities to attend. The tradition has continued unbroken for five centuries, making it one of the oldest continuously held religious gatherings in the Himalayas. In recent years, the Himachal Pradesh government has promoted the Mandi Shivaratri as a state-level festival, adding cultural programmes, craft fairs, and tourism infrastructure — a familiar pattern of state co-option of religious festivals.

Kashmiri Herath: A Distinctive Shaiva Tradition

For Kashmiri Pandits, Shivaratri — called Herath (from "Hara-ratri," the night of Hara/Shiva) — is the most important festival of the year, celebrated with distinctive rituals that differ significantly from the pan-Hindu observance. The festival spans 21 days, with the core ritual centred on a vatuk — a clay pot symbolising Shiva — filled with walnuts and water, and worshipped over several days with offerings of flowers, incense, and special foods.

The walnuts have a specific ritual symbolism: they represent the four Vedas (the four segments of the walnut), and their distribution as prasad on the day after Herath is a gesture of community solidarity. The Kashmiri Pandit Herath cuisine includes nadur yakhni (lotus stem in yoghurt gravy), dum aloo, and tserith chutney (sheep's sorrel chutney). Following the mass displacement of Kashmiri Pandits from the Kashmir Valley in 1990, Herath has become a crucial marker of community identity in exile, celebrated in Jammu, Delhi, and diaspora communities worldwide.

Displacement and ritual continuity: The Kashmiri Pandit diaspora — estimated at over 300,000 — maintains Herath with remarkable fidelity. The festival has become a vehicle for intergenerational transmission of Kashmiri Shaiva culture. Community organisations in Delhi, Jammu, and overseas hold collective Herath celebrations, and the distribution of walnuts as prasad carries an added layer of meaning as a gesture of community survival.

Bhang and Shivaratri: Ritual Intoxicant

Shivaratri is one of the few occasions in India where the consumption of bhang (a cannabis preparation, typically mixed with milk, almonds, and spices into a drink called thandai) is legally and culturally tolerated in certain locations. This tolerance is rooted in Shiva's mythological association with cannabis — devotional texts describe him as a regular consumer of bhang, and the plant is considered sacred to him. In Varanasi and Ujjain during Shivaratri, government-authorised bhang shops operate openly, serving bhang thandai to throngs of devotees and ascetics.

Bhang is legally tolerated at Varanasi and Ujjain during Shivaratri under the provisions of the Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act (1985), which exempts cannabis leaves and seeds (bhang) from its prohibitions on cannabis resin (charas) and flowering tops (ganja). This legal distinction — between bhang and other cannabis preparations — dates from the colonial era and reflects the British decision not to ban a substance so culturally embedded in North Indian religious practice. An estimated 10,000+ litres of bhang thandai are consumed in Varanasi alone during Shivaratri.

The association of Shiva with cannabis is well-attested in medieval devotional texts, but some reformist Hindu organisations contest it, arguing that it misrepresents the deity and encourages substance use. The tension mirrors larger debates within Hinduism about the boundary between tantric and orthodox practice.

The Twelve Jyotirlingas

The twelve Jyotirlinga temples form a sacred geography that structures pilgrimage and devotion for millions of Shaivas. The list is standardised but not entirely ancient — the concept of twelve Jyotirlingas crystallised in the medieval period, and different texts give slightly different lists. Fun Facts

At the Isha Yoga Centre in Coimbatore, Sadhguru Jaggi Vasudev's organisation hosts an all-night Shivaratri celebration that is arguably the most globally streamed Hindu religious event, drawing an online audience of over 10 million across 40+ countries — a 21st-century media phenomenon built around an ancient vigil tradition.

The Nepalese celebration of Shivaratri at the Pashupatinath temple in Kathmandu draws over a million devotees from India and Nepal. Sadhus — including the famously photogenic Naga sadhus, covered in ash and dreadlocks — converge on the temple complex, creating a visual spectacle that has made Pashupatinath during Shivaratri one of the most photographed religious events in South Asia.

The bilva (bael) leaf used in Shiva worship is trifoliate — its three leaflets are believed to represent Shiva's three eyes, or the trident (trishul). Botanically, Aegle marmelos is a tree native to India with documented medicinal properties, used in Ayurveda for digestive disorders. During Shivaratri, the demand for bilva leaves in temple cities creates a seasonal economy employing thousands of leaf-gatherers.

Artwork References

Chola Bronze Nataraja (12th century) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. The V&A holds one of the finest Chola-period bronze Natarajas: Shiva as Lord of the Dance, encircled by a ring of fire, right foot crushing the demon of ignorance, left leg raised in grace. This four-armed, perfectly balanced bronze is one of the most iconic images in all of Indian art. The Nataraja iconography is inextricably linked to Shivaratri, the night of the cosmic dance.

Chola Bronze Nataraja (11th–12th century) — British Museum, London. Another masterwork of the Chola bronze tradition, showing similar iconography with subtle variations in proportion and detail, demonstrating the variations possible within the canonical form.

Ellora Kailasa Temple (8th century) — Maharashtra, UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Kailasa temple (Cave 16) at Ellora is the world's largest monolithic structure — carved top-down from a single basalt cliff. Dedicated to Shiva, the temple complex features narrative panels of Shiva's marriage, the Ravana-shaking-Kailash episode, and the Tandava dance. The carving's audacity — removing an estimated 200,000 tonnes of rock — remains unmatched in Indian rock-cut architecture.
