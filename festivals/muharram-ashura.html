Muharram & Ashura

Commemorating the Martyrdom of Imam Hussain
☪️ Muslim (Shia — also observed by many Sunnis and some Hindus in India) 📍 Major centres: Lucknow, Hyderabad, Kolkata, Delhi, Kashmir, Mumbai 📅 Muharram 1–10 (Ashura is the 10th day); Islamic lunar calendar ⏳ Commemorated in India since at least the 14th century

Historical Background

Muharram is the first month of the Islamic calendar. Its 10th day, Ashura, marks the Battle of Karbala (680 CE / 61 AH), where Imam Hussain ibn Ali — the grandson of Prophet Muhammad — and 72 of his followers were killed by the forces of the Umayyad caliph Yazid. For Shia Muslims worldwide, Hussain's stand — a small group refusing to pledge allegiance to what they saw as an unjust ruler, knowing they would be killed — is the central narrative of sacrifice, justice, and resistance against tyranny.

In India, Shia Islam arrived with the Delhi Sultanate and flourished under the Shia-ruled kingdoms of the Deccan (Bijapur, Golconda) and Awadh (Lucknow). The Nawabs of Awadh made Lucknow the greatest centre of Shia Muharram observance outside Persia. The city's imambaras (congregational halls), built specifically for Muharram, are architectural masterpieces — the Bara Imambara (1784) is one of the largest vaulted halls in the world without external support beams.

Indian Muharram Traditions

Taziya Processions

The taziya is a replica of Imam Hussain's tomb at Karbala, typically made of bamboo, paper, and foil. Elaborately decorated, these are carried in procession and then immersed in water or buried. The taziya tradition is uniquely Indian — it does not exist in Arab Shia practice — and evolved from the Hindu practice of idol immersion (visarjan). The cross-pollination is so evident that some scholars call taziya-making "Muharram's Durga Puja moment." In parts of Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, Hindu artisans are the primary taziya-makers, and some Hindus participate in the processions.

Marsiya & Soz

Marsiya is elegiac poetry recounting the tragedy of Karbala. Mir Anis (1803–1874) and Mirza Dabeer elevated marsiya to high literature in Lucknow, composing thousands of verses. Soz is the musical recitation — a cappella, deeply mournful — performed at majlis (gatherings). The Lucknow gharana of marsiya recitation is considered among the finest poetic traditions in Urdu literature.

Matam (Self-Flagellation)

Matam — ritual chest-beating and, in some communities, self-flagellation with blades (zanjir zani) — is the most controversial Muharram practice. Many Shia religious authorities have discouraged or banned bloodletting, and it is illegal in several countries. In India, it persists in some communities (notably in parts of Lucknow, Hyderabad, and Mumbai), though the trend is toward blood donation drives as an alternative — "donate blood, not shed it" campaigns have gained traction in recent years.

Regional Variations

RegionDistinctive Feature
Lucknow (UP)Heart of Indian Shia practice. Bara Imambara, Chhota Imambara. World-class marsiya recitation. The nawabi Muharram tradition includes the ritual of sham-e-ghariban (evening of the homeless) — lamps lit in memory of the Karbala survivors.
Hyderabad (Telangana)Bibi-ka-Alam procession — one of the oldest in India, dating to the Qutb Shahi period. Distinctive Deccani Muharram cuisine: haleem is central. Women's majlis are particularly strong in Hyderabadi Shia culture.
KashmirMuharram in Kashmir has a distinctive character, blending Persian influences with Kashmiri folk traditions. The 10th of Muharram is a state holiday. Processions in Srinagar's old city.
KolkataMetcalfe Hall area and Zakaria Street see large Muharram processions. The city's syncretic tradition includes Hindu participation in some areas.
MumbaiDongri, Bhendi Bazaar, and other Muslim-majority areas host large processions. The city's diverse Muslim population includes Sunni participation in Muharram observance.
Bihar / UP (rural)Hindu-Muslim syncretic participation: in some villages, Hindus make taziyas, observe fasts, and join processions. This "Ganga-Jamuni tehzeeb" is under strain from political polarisation.

Fun Facts & Things of Note

The Bara Imambara in Lucknow, built in 1784 by Nawab Asaf-ud-Daula, is an architectural wonder: its central hall (50m × 15m × 15m) has no pillars supporting the ceiling. It was built as a famine-relief project — the nawab employed thousands during a devastating famine, and nobles who wanted to appear wealthy during the day demolished parts at night so work would continue.
In parts of Trinidad and Guyana, the indenture diaspora includes a syncretic Muharram tradition known as "Hosay" — a creolised form blending Shia, Hindu, and Afro-Caribbean elements. The taziyas became "tadjahs." During the 1884 Hosay massacre in Trinidad, British colonial forces shot at participants — one of the bloodiest events in Caribbean Indian history.
The Indian Shia tradition of marsiya poetry is so distinctive that an entire genre of Urdu literature — the musaddas (six-line stanza form) — was effectively perfected by Mir Anis for Karbala narratives. Some of his marsiyas run to over 200 stanzas, performed over multiple sessions.

Artwork & References

Mughal and Awadhi Miniatures (18th–19th c.) — The British Museum and Chester Beatty Library hold paintings of Muharram processions, including taziya processions with decorated structures. The Victoria and Albert Museum has Company School paintings of Muharram in Lucknow and Delhi.
Lucknow Imambaras — Architectural Heritage — The Bara Imambara and Chhota Imambara in Lucknow are some of India's finest examples of late Mughal/Awadhi architecture. Extensive photographic documentation exists.
Marsiya Manuscripts — Libraries in Lucknow and Hyderabad hold manuscript collections of Mir Anis and Mirza Dabeer's poetry. The Rampur Raza Library is a major repository.
