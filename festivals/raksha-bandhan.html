Raksha Bandhan

Rakhi · Avani Avittam · Janai Purnima — The protective thread that ties together family, history, and contested legend

Religion: Hindu, Sikh, Jain, secular Key Regions: Pan-Indian (North and West India principally) Timing: Shravana Purnima (Jul–Aug)

Current Practices

Raksha Bandhan is centred on the ritual of a sister tying a sacred thread (rakhi) around her brother's right wrist, applying a tilak (vermillion mark) on his forehead, performing an aarti, and offering sweets. The brother, in turn, gives a gift (monetary or material) and pledges protection. This core ritual — simple, portable, and emotionally resonant — has made Raksha Bandhan one of India's most widely observed festivals, easily adapted across religious and regional boundaries.

The rakhi itself has undergone a dramatic material transformation. Traditionally a simple cotton or silk thread dyed with turmeric and sometimes strung with a single bead or flower, the rakhi market now encompasses an extraordinary range: mass-produced decorative rakhis starting at ₹5, designer silk-and-zari rakhis, rakhis embedded with cartoon characters for children, rakhis with religious motifs (Om, swastika, Guru Nanak), and even diamond-studded rakhis sold by luxury jewellers for sums exceeding ₹5 lakh.

The rakhi thread market in India is estimated at over ₹4,000 crore (roughly $480 million), making it one of the country's largest single-festival retail categories. Millions of rakhis are exported annually to the Indian diaspora, particularly to the UK, USA, Canada, UAE, and Australia — creating a festival-driven global supply chain.

The festival also encompasses the Brahminical ritual of Upakarma (also called Avani Avittam in Tamil Nadu and Janai Purnima in Nepal), in which Brahmin men change their sacred thread (yajnopavita) after ritual purification. The coincidence of these two observances — the sibling thread and the sacred thread — on the same full moon is not accidental but reflects a shared symbolic grammar of threads as bonds, both social and cosmic.

Historical Origins and the Karnavati-Humayun Legend

The history of Raksha Bandhan is more fluid and multi-faceted than its modern framing as a sibling festival suggests. In the Vedic period, the tying of protective threads (raksha sutra) was a priestly ritual performed for kings and patrons before battle or during auspicious ceremonies — not limited to sibling relationships. The Bhavishya Purana (c. 12th–15th centuries) describes the ritual of tying a protective amulet during Shravana, and the practice had associations with warding off disease during the monsoon season.

"On the full-moon day of the month of Shravana, after due performance of the upakarman rite, a Brahmana should tie a charmed thread round the wrist of a Kshatriya king." — Bhavishya Purana, Madhyama Parva

The most famous — and most contested — historical rakhi story is the Karnavati-Humayun legend: Rani Karnavati, the widowed queen of Chittor, supposedly sent a rakhi to the Mughal emperor Humayun in 1535, appealing for protection against Bahadur Shah of Gujarat. Humayun, according to the tale, was so moved by this gesture that he abandoned his military campaign and marched to Chittor's defence (arriving too late, as Karnavati and the women of Chittor had already performed jauhar — mass self-immolation).

The Karnavati-Humayun rakhi story appears in no contemporary Mughal or Rajput sources. It first surfaces in colonial-era texts, notably James Tod's Annals and Antiquities of Rajasthan (1829), a text whose romanticised view of Rajput history has been extensively critiqued by modern historians. The story likely serves a 19th-century political purpose: constructing a narrative of Hindu-Muslim solidarity that appealed to both colonial administrators and Indian nationalists.

Rabindranath Tagore deployed the Karnavati-Humayun story during the 1905 Bengal Partition agitation, organising rakhi-tying ceremonies between Hindus and Muslims as a gesture of communal solidarity. Tagore's strategic use of the rakhi — transforming it from a sibling or protective thread into a symbol of inter-community bonding — illustrates the festival's remarkable symbolic elasticity.

Diaspora Adaptations

Raksha Bandhan has adapted remarkably well to diaspora conditions. The ritual's simplicity — a thread, a sweet, a gift — requires no temple, no priest, no large gathering, making it fully portable to apartment living rooms in London, New Jersey, or Singapore. The postal service and later courier services became essential infrastructure: India Post handles an estimated 30 million+ rakhi deliveries each year, and in the pre-internet era, a rakhi arriving by post weeks in advance was the typical diaspora experience.

The festival has also been creatively reimagined in diaspora contexts. Environmental groups have promoted "Rakhi for Trees" campaigns, tying rakhis to trees as a pledge of protection. Feminist re-workings of the festival have sisters tying rakhis on sisters, or framing the festival as a mutual rather than patriarchal protection bond. These adaptations — and the ease with which Raksha Bandhan accommodates them — demonstrate the festival's conceptual openness.

Modern Tensions

Raksha Bandhan has been subject to feminist critique for encoding a patriarchal relationship: the sister actively performs submission (tying the thread, performing aarti) in exchange for male protection and material compensation. The ritual's script — sister needs protection, brother provides it — sits uneasily with contemporary gender politics. In response, many families have adopted mutual rakhi-tying (siblings tie rakhis on each other) or reframed the ritual as one of mutual care rather than gendered protection.

Commercialisation has also drawn criticism. The transformation of a simple turmeric-dyed thread into a luxury commodity (diamond rakhis, branded designer rakhis, rakhis embedded with gold coins) arguably hollows out the ritual's symbolic meaning. The festival now generates a month-long marketing build-up in Indian retail, with rakhis competing for shelf space alongside sweets, gift hampers, and greeting cards — a commercial ecology indistinguishable from Western holidays like Mother's Day or Valentine's Day.

The Tagore-era tradition of tying rakhis across communities as a gesture of Hindu-Muslim unity has been revived periodically (most recently during the anti-CAA protests of 2019–20) but is contested by Hindu nationalist groups who argue that the rakhi's "original" meaning is specifically Hindu and brother-sister. This debate over the festival's "authentic" meaning is itself a modern political phenomenon.

Fun Facts

India Post handles an estimated 30 million rakhi-specific deliveries each August, making Raksha Bandhan one of the largest drivers of domestic postal traffic in the country. Special Raksha Bandhan envelopes and express delivery schemes are launched annually.

Rakhis range from ₹5 mass-produced cotton threads to diamond-studded rakhis sold by luxury jewellers for over ₹5 lakh. In 2019, a Mumbai jeweller created a rakhi with 18k gold, diamonds, and a miniature Ganesha that was priced at ₹7 lakh.

In Nepal, Janai Purnima involves not only rakhi-tying but the consumption of kwati — a soup made from nine types of sprouted beans, considered highly nutritious and specifically suited to the monsoon season when digestive strength is believed to be weaker. The dish represents a traditional nutritional wisdom embedded in festival cuisine.

Artwork References

Mughal Miniature: Rakhi-Tying Ceremony (18th century) — National Museum, New Delhi. A delicate miniature showing a rakhi-tying scene, likely commissioned by a Rajput court under Mughal suzerainty. The painting demonstrates the cross-community adoption of the rakhi ritual in pre-modern India, with its Mughal artistic style depicting a non-Mughal ceremony.

Rajasthani Miniature: Festival of Rakhi (19th century) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. A vibrant court scene showing rakhi celebrations in a Rajput palace, with attending courtiers and musicians. The painting provides visual evidence of the festival's status as a courtly as well as domestic observance.

Company School Painting: Brahmin Thread Ceremony (c. 1810) — British Museum, London. A detailed Company School watercolour depicting the Upakarma / sacred thread changing ceremony on Shravana Purnima, commissioned by British East India Company officials. The painting documents the Brahminical counterpart to the rakhi festival.
