Onam

Thiruvonam — Kerala's harvest festival where the egalitarian King Mahabali returns, celebrated by Hindus, Muslims, and Christians alike

Religion: Hindu (but celebrated by all Kerala communities) Key Regions: Kerala; global Malayali diaspora Timing: Chingam month (Aug–Sep); Thiruvonam nakshatra

Current Practices

Onam is Kerala's defining cultural festival — a ten-day celebration whose scale and inclusiveness have made it the state's de facto national festival. The festival's centrepiece is Thiruvonam, the most auspicious day, when the mythical King Mahabali (Maveli) is believed to visit his former kingdom. Malayali homes prepare elaborate flower carpets (pookalam) at their entrances to welcome the returning king, and families gather for the grand Onam feast — the sadya.

The Onam sadya is one of the world's great culinary rituals: a vegetarian banquet served on a fresh banana leaf, with dishes arranged in a specific spatial order. A standard sadya includes 26 items, though temple feasts can reach 64 items. The meal follows a precise sequence — pickles and chips first (upper left of the leaf), then thoran and mezhukkupuratti (stir-fried vegetables), followed by the liquid dishes (sambar, rasam, moru), with payasam (sweet pudding) concluding the meal. The final act of folding the banana leaf — towards oneself to indicate satisfaction, away to indicate dissatisfaction — is a subtle semiotic ritual.

An Onam sadya at major Kerala temples can feature up to 64 items — a number that represents the 64 traditional art forms (Kalaripayattu, Kathakali, etc.) of Kerala. The Aranmula temple sadya is particularly famous, served to thousands of devotees on Thiruvonam day. Some temple sadyas use up to 300 kg of rice, 500 kg of vegetables, and 200 litres of payasam in a single day's service.

The Mahabali Myth: An Egalitarian Kingdom

The mythological framework of Onam sets it apart from most Hindu festivals. The story concerns Mahabali (Maveli), an asura (demon) king who ruled Kerala in an era of perfect justice, prosperity, and equality. Threatened by Mahabali's growing power, the gods sent Vishnu in his Vamana (dwarf brahmin) avatar, who requested three paces of land from the charitable king. When Mahabali granted the request, Vamana grew to cosmic size, covering the earth and heavens in two steps. For the third step, Mahabali offered his own head — an act of devotion that won him Vishnu's blessing to return to his kingdom once a year. That annual return is Onam.

"When Mahabali ruled the land, all men were equal, there was no theft, no falsehood, no envy. People lived in contentment without sorrow or disease." — Traditional Onam song (Onappaattu), 19th century or earlier

The Mahabali myth is remarkable in several respects. First, the protagonist is an asura — typically the antagonist in Hindu mythology — who is memorialised as a just and beloved ruler. Second, the myth encodes a vision of an egalitarian, caste-free society ("no one locked their doors") that many scholars read as folk memory of a pre-Brahminical social order in Kerala. Third, the myth's structure — the king is tricked by the gods but rewarded with annual return — has been interpreted as a narrative of Brahminical absorption of a pre-existing Dravidian or indigenous ruler-cult.

Mahabali's kingdom is described in Malayalam folklore as so egalitarian that "no one locked their doors." The traditional Onam song (Maveli Naadu Vaneedum Kaalam) describes an era where "all people were equal, there was no poverty, no sorrow, no disease, and no child mortality." This utopian vision is invoked annually during Onam — a rare example of a festival that ritually commemorates an imagined egalitarian past.

A Cross-Religion Festival

Onam's status as a festival celebrated by all Kerala communities — Hindu, Muslim, Christian, and (to a lesser extent) Jewish — is one of its most distinctive features. Kerala's Syrian Christians, who trace their origins to the 1st century CE, celebrate Onam with sadyas, pookalams, and new clothes just as their Hindu neighbours do. Kerala Muslims, particularly in the Malabar region, participate in Onam celebrations, though with some variation (the Onam sadya in Muslim households may substitute beef dishes for some vegetarian items but retains the banana-leaf format).

This cross-religious participation is possible because Onam's mythological narrative (the Mahabali story) operates largely independently of Hindu temple ritual. There is no puja, no priest, no deity installation required — only the flower carpet, the feast, and the shared cultural memory of a just king. The Kerala government explicitly promotes Onam as a secular state festival, with official celebrations, school holidays, and tourism marketing that emphasises its cross-community character.

Some Hindu nationalist groups in Kerala have attempted to re-Hinduise Onam, arguing that Mahabali is a Hindu king and that Onam is fundamentally a Vamana Jayanti (the day Vishnu in Vamana avatar suppressed Mahabali) rather than a celebration of Mahabali's return. This reading — which inverts the festival's emotional logic — has gained limited traction but illustrates the ongoing political contestation over India's religious festivals.

The Nehru Trophy and Snake-Boat Races

The Vallam Kali (snake-boat race) is Onam's most spectacular sporting event, and the Nehru Trophy Boat Race on Kerala's Punnamada Lake is its crown jewel. The boats — chundan vallams — are 100–138 feet long, manned by over 100 rowers sitting two abreast, with four helmsmen and 25 singers. The rowers, synchronised by the rhythm of boat songs (vanchipattu), propel these enormous wooden vessels at surprising speeds.

The Nehru Trophy Boat Race draws over 100,000 spectators to the banks of Punnamada Lake — a crowd larger than many international sporting events. The event was initiated in 1952 when Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru, visiting Kerala, was given an impromptu snake-boat race and was so impressed that he donated a trophy on the spot. The trophy, a silver replica of a snake-boat, has been contested annually ever since.

The snake-boats themselves represent an extraordinary craft tradition. Each boat is carved from a single anjili (wild jack) tree trunk, treated with fish oil and vegetable resins, and maintained by village communities. The boat's design — long, narrow, with a raised prow shaped like a cobra's hood — has remained essentially unchanged for centuries. The singing tradition — the vanchipattu — is an art form in its own right, with songs that praise the boat, its village, and the rowers' strength, passed down orally within rowing communities.

Pookalam: Floral Geometry

The pookalam (flower carpet) is Onam's visual signature — a circular, multi-coloured design created at the entrance of homes using fresh flower petals. The pookalam grows in complexity through the ten days of Onam: on Atham (day one), a simple, small circle is created using a single variety of flower (typically thumba — white Leucas flowers); each day adds a new ring, layer, and flower variety, culminating in an elaborate geometric mandala on Thiruvonam.

Pookalam competitions — in schools, offices, residential associations, and as televised events — have driven the tradition toward increasing technical sophistication. Top competitors use stencils, colour-graded petals, and designs that incorporate cultural themes (Kathakali faces, snake-boats, temple architecture). A serious competitive pookalam can take a team of 5–10 people 4–6 hours to complete, using over 50 varieties of flowers. The flower market for Onam is a major seasonal economy, with an estimated 10,000+ tonnes of flowers sold in Kerala's markets during the festival week.

Historical Origins

Onam's origins predate Hinduism's penetration into Kerala. Most scholars agree the festival represents a Dravidian harvest festival later overlaid with the Vaishnava Vamana-Mahabali myth — a classic example of syncretic absorption of pre-existing traditions into the Hindu fold. The Sangam-era Tamil text Maduraikkanchi (c. 2nd–4th century CE) references a harvest festival in the Tamilakam region (which included present-day Kerala) that shares features with Onam.

The festival's formal structure — the ten-day duration, the specific flower rituals, and the Thiruvonam culmination — crystallised during the medieval period under the patronage of the Chera and later Travancore kingdoms. The Travancore kings, particularly during the 18th–19th centuries, promoted Onam as a state festival, incorporating elements of the royal durbar and military display that have since faded from popular practice.

The largest state-organised cultural festival: The Kerala government's "Onam Week" celebrations — with official events in every district, a state-level inauguration, tourism promotion campaigns, and a week of public holidays — represent the largest state-organised cultural festival in India. Since 1961, the state has formally celebrated Onam as Kerala's national festival, a status that predates many of the nation-building festivals of independent India.

Fun Facts

The Kerala government's Onam Week is the largest state-organised cultural festival in India, featuring thousands of events across all 14 districts — pookalam competitions, boat races, Kathakali performances, martial arts demonstrations (Kalaripayattu), and over 100 official stages for cultural programmes.

The traditional Onam sadya sweet, palada pradhaman, uses a specific variety of rice (matta rice flakes) cooked in milk and sugar for up to six hours — reducing 5 litres of milk to about 2 litres — in a slow evaporation process that gives the dish its distinctive pinkish-brown colour and condensed-milk depth.

Malayali diaspora communities in the Gulf — where an estimated 3.5 million Keralites live — celebrate Onam with extraordinary enthusiasm. In Dubai, Onam sadyas are served to tens of thousands in community halls and restaurants, and the UAE government has occasionally granted special event permits for boat-race demonstrations on Dubai Creek.

Artwork References

Raja Ravi Varma: Mahabali Paintings (late 19th century) — Various collections. India's most celebrated academic painter produced several works depicting the Vamana-Mahabali encounter, including the famous composition of Vamana placing his foot on Mahabali's head as the king bows in devotion. Ravi Varma's oleographs of this scene were among his most popular prints and helped standardise the visual iconography of the myth.

Kerala Mural: Vamana Avatar (17th–18th century) — Mattancherry Palace (Dutch Palace), Kochi. The palace's extraordinary cycle of Kerala mural paintings includes a panel depicting the Vamana avatar, with Mahabali shown as a dignified, regal figure — a characterisation consistent with the Onam festival's positive view of the asura king. The Kerala mural tradition, with its distinctive ochre-reds, greenish-blues, and bold outlines, is one of India's major painting schools.

Company School Painting: Snake-Boat Race (c. 1820) — British Library, London. A watercolour commissioned by a British East India Company resident in Travancore, showing a snake-boat race on the Ashtamudi Lake — one of the earliest European visual records of the tradition.
