Christmas in India

Bada Din — One of the world's oldest continuous Christian traditions, dating to the 4th century in Kerala, predating Christmas celebrations across most of Europe

Religion: Christian (Catholic, Protestant, Syrian Orthodox, various denominations) Key Regions: Kerala, Goa, Northeast India, Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata, Chennai Timing: December 25 (Western); January 7 (Orthodox)

Christianity in India Before Christianity Reached Europe

The most startling fact about Christmas in India is the antiquity of the Christian tradition it represents. The Kerala Syrian Christian community (Nasrani) traces its origins to St. Thomas the Apostle, who, according to ancient tradition, arrived on the Malabar Coast in 52 CE — making Indian Christianity older than the Christianisation of most of Europe. By the 4th century CE, when the Roman Empire was still persecuting Christians, Kerala's Syrian Christians were likely already celebrating the feast of the Nativity in their churches. This means Christmas observances in India predate Christmas traditions in Britain, Germany, and Scandinavia by centuries.

The Kerala Syrian Christian community may have been celebrating Christmas since the 4th century CE — predating Christmas traditions in Britain (where Christianity became dominant only after the 6th–7th centuries), Germany (where mass conversion occurred under Charlemagne in the 8th–9th centuries), and Scandinavia (which was not fully Christianised until the 12th century) by centuries. India's Christmas is not an imported colonial festival — it is one of the oldest continuous Christmas traditions in the world.

"The church in India was founded by St. Thomas the Apostle, who suffered martyrdom and was buried at Mylapore. In the 4th century, the church of Malabar was in communion with the church of Persia, and observed the feasts of the Nativity and Epiphany according to the East Syrian rite." — Traveller's account of the Kerala Christians, recorded by Cosmas Indicopleustes, 6th century CE

The Kerala Christian community preserved East Syriac liturgical traditions for centuries, including the celebration of Yeldo (Syriac for Nativity). The distinctive Kerala Christmas incorporates indigenous elements: the use of nilavilakku (brass oil lamps) instead of candles, the serving of appam and chicken stew at the Christmas feast, and the decoration of homes with banana and mango leaves alongside the more familiar star lanterns. In many Nasrani homes, the fast of 25 days (from December 1 to Christmas Eve) — abstaining from meat, fish, dairy, and alcohol — precedes the Christmas feast, a practice derived from the East Syriac Nativity Fast.

Goa's Portuguese Christmas

Christmas in Goa bears the deep imprint of over 450 years of Portuguese rule (1510–1961). The Portuguese introduced not only Roman Catholic liturgy but a distinctive festive culture: the Missa de Galo (Rooster's Mass) at midnight on Christmas Eve, named for the folk belief that a rooster crowed at midnight to announce Christ's birth; elaborate crib-making (presepio), with competitions between neighbourhoods and parishes; and a culinary tradition that represents one of the world's most extended examples of cultural fusion.

The Goan Christmas sweet platter (kuswar / consoada) is a marvel of Indo-Portuguese gastronomy: bebinca (a layered coconut-and-egg pudding, with up to 16 layers, each individually broiled — a dish that takes hours to prepare), dodol (a jaggery-and-coconut toffee made with rice flour), neureos (crescent-shaped pastries stuffed with coconut and semolina), kulkuls (crispy, sweet dough curls), and batica (coconut cake). The traditional number of sweets in kuswar is 21 — a number with symbolic significance — and the preparation begins weeks in advance, occupying the women of Goan Catholic households in a labour of culinary devotion. The Goan Christmas lunch centres on sorpotel (a spicy pork stew of Portuguese origin, radically Indianised with vinegar, chillies, and Goan spice paste) and sannas (steamed rice cakes).

Catholic-Protestant diversity: Indian Christianity encompasses the full denominational spectrum — from the ancient Syrian Orthodox and Catholic traditions of Kerala, through the Portuguese-influenced Catholicism of Goa, to the Baptist, Presbyterian, and Pentecostal denominations that dominate in the Northeast. Christmas observances differ accordingly: Syriac churches use the Julian calendar (Christmas on January 7), Catholics attend Midnight Mass, and Protestant communities emphasise carol services and nativity plays. The diversity confounds any attempt to describe a single "Indian Christmas."

Northeastern Christmas: Tribal Voices

The three majority-Christian states of Northeast India — Nagaland, Mizoram, and Meghalaya — celebrate Christmas with a distinctive character shaped by their relatively recent Christianisation (largely 19th–20th century, through British and American Baptist and Presbyterian missionaries). Unlike Kerala's ancient Christianity or Goa's colonial Catholicism, Northeastern Christianity is predominantly Protestant, and its Christmas traditions reflect the enthusiastic adoption and indigenisation of Western missionary practices.

In Nagaland, Christmas begins weeks in advance with intense choir practice — every Baptist church in the state prepares special Christmas cantatas. Village churches are elaborately decorated with locally sourced materials: bamboo, wild flowers, pine branches, and hand-stitched cloth banners. The Christmas feast is served communally in church halls after the morning service, with villages pooling resources to ensure that every household — including the poorest — has a full meal. The Naga song tradition, deeply musical and polyphonic, produces Christmas carols sung in Angami, Ao, Lotha, and other Naga languages — melodies that predate Christianisation, now fused with Christian lyrics in a distinctive form of indigenous worship music.

Some scholars and activists have critiqued the Northeastern Christianisation as a form of cultural erasure, noting that pre-Christian Naga rituals and festivals (many tied to headhunting and animist practices) were suppressed by missionaries and are now being "reclaimed" through modern festivals like Hornbill. Others, including a majority of Naga Christians, view the adoption of Christianity as an act of cultural self-determination that allowed Nagas to navigate colonial and postcolonial modernity without absorption into Hindu-majority culture. The Hornbill Festival's co-existence alongside Christmas — both celebrated in December — embodies this unresolved tension.

Secularisation and Consumer Christmas in Metropolitan India

In India's major cities — particularly Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, and Kolkata — Christmas has undergone the same secularisation and commercialisation observed globally. Shopping malls erect Christmas trees and deploy staff in Santa suits; bakeries sell plum cakes (a British colonial legacy adapted with Indian spices); and interfaith participation in Christmas celebrations is common. The Hindi term Bada Din ("Big Day") captures the secular, festive character that Christmas has acquired in urban India — a day of celebration, eating, and shopping that is not exclusively Christian.

Kolkata's Park Street Christmas lights — a tradition inherited from the city's Anglo-Indian community and sustained by commercial interests — draw hundreds of thousands of visitors, the vast majority of them Hindu. The Allahabad (Prayagraj) Christmas cake tradition dates back to British-era bakeries that developed a distinctive spiced fruitcake combining English plum cake technique with Indian ingredients (local rum, indigenous spices). These fusion traditions — the product of two centuries of colonial and postcolonial cultural exchange — represent Christmas's Indianisation in its most complete form.

The Allahabad (Prayagraj) Christmas cake tradition dates back to 19th-century British-era bakeries that developed a distinctive spiced fruitcake. Unlike the heavier, lard-based English plum cake, the Allahabad cake uses local rum, spices (cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves from Kerala), and candied fruits (tutti-frutti from Mumbai). These cakes are aged for weeks — some bakeries soak them in rum for a full month before Christmas — and their production represents a culinary tradition that has passed from British bakers to Muslim and Hindu halwais (confectioners). Some of these bakeries, still operating under their original 19th-century names, ship cakes across India and to the diaspora.

Fun Facts

India has approximately 28 million Christians — roughly the population of Australia. Yet Indian Christians comprise only 2.3% of India's population. This minority status creates a distinctive Christmas dynamic: in regions like Kerala and the Northeast, Christmas is a public, majority celebration; in most of India, it is a minority festival celebrated within a dominant Hindu culture — a situation that has fostered creative syncretism rather than competition.

The Varginha (Virgin) cheese, a mild, fresh cheese used in Goan Christmas sweets, is made by curdling milk with vinegar — a technique of Portuguese origin. Goan Catholic households still make this cheese from scratch for Christmas, continuing a tradition that has been lost in many Portuguese households in Portugal itself, where industrial cheese production has replaced home preparation.

Artwork References

Indo-Portuguese Ivory Madonna (17th century) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. A striking ivory statuette of the Virgin and Child, carved in Goa under Portuguese patronage, combining European Marian iconography with Indian craftsmanship, materials (ivory from Kerala's elephants), and stylistic elements (the Madonna's facial features show Indian influence). The V&A holds an important collection of these Goan ivories, which represent one of the earliest sustained traditions of Christian art in Asia.

Christian Mughal Miniature: The Nativity (c. 1600) — British Museum, London. A remarkable miniature painting produced in the Mughal court atelier, possibly at the direction of Emperor Akbar or Jahangir, showing the Nativity scene in the Mughal miniature style — Mary and Joseph in Mughal dress, the Magi as Mughal courtiers, and the stable transformed into a Mughal pavilion. This painting demonstrates the Mughal court's genuine curiosity about Christianity and its willingness to incorporate Christian subjects into the imperial artistic repertoire.

Mughal Madonna and Child (c. 1590) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. Another Mughal-period painting showing the Virgin and Child in a recognisably European composition but executed with Mughal techniques, likely copied from a European engraving brought by Jesuit missionaries to the Mughal court.
