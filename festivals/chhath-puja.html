Chhath Puja

Surya Shashti · Dala Chhath — The four-day sun ritual led by women, carried by migration from Bihar to the world's megacities

Religion: Hindu (but with pre-Vedic solar worship roots) Key Regions: Bihar, Eastern UP, Jharkhand, Nepal Terai; diaspora hubs (Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, Gulf, Caribbean, Mauritius) Timing: Kartik Shukla Shashti (Oct–Nov); also Chaitra Chhath (Mar–Apr)

Current Practices

Chhath Puja is one of India's most rigorous festivals — a four-day sequence of fasting, ritual bathing, and sun worship that demands extraordinary physical discipline. It is also the only major Hindu festival that is women-led: the primary votary (parvaitin) is almost always a woman, and the festival's oral tradition, ritual knowledge, and transmission are overwhelmingly female domains. The male role is largely supportive — arranging logistics, cleaning ghats, and providing material for offerings.

The four-day structure is precisely defined:

Day 1 — Nahay Khay: The parvaitin bathes in a river or water body, then prepares a simple meal of kaddu-bhat (pumpkin and rice) cooked in a clay oven, from which she eats first, and only then do other family members eat.

Day 2 — Kharna: A day-long waterless fast is broken after sunset with a meal of roti and kheer (rice pudding) made with jaggery, prepared on a clay oven. After this meal, the 36-hour nirjala (waterless) fast begins.

Day 3 — Sandhya Arghya: The parvaitin, family members, and community proceed to the riverbank in the evening, where she stands in the water — often waist-deep — and offers arghya (water and milk offerings) to the setting sun. This inversion — offering to the setting rather than rising sun — is unique to Chhath.

Day 4 — Usha Arghya: The same ritual is performed at dawn for the rising sun, after which the fast is broken.

The offerings (soop) are carried in large bamboo baskets and include thekua (a wheat-flour-and-jaggery cookie fried in ghee), seasonal fruits (especially sugarcane, bananas, and coconuts), and whole turmeric plants. The visual spectacle of thousands of parvaitins standing in the water, holding soop aloft, with lamps flickering in the twilight, is among the most striking of any Indian festival.

The thekua — Chhath's signature prasad — is essentially a 2,000+ year old cookie recipe: wheat flour, jaggery, and ghee, pressed into a wooden mould and deep-fried. Unlike modern cookies, thekua contains no leavening agents, no refined sugar, and no preservatives — and yet, when properly made, it remains edible for weeks. The recipe is remarkably consistent across Bihar, UP, Jharkhand, and the diaspora, with only minor regional variations in spice (cardamom vs. fennel).

Historical Origins

Chhath's origins lie in the pre-Vedic sun-worship traditions of the Gangetic plain. The Rig Veda contains hymns to Surya (the sun god) and Usha (the dawn goddess), and the ritual of standing in water to offer arghya to the sun has identifiable Vedic parallels. However, the specific Chhath ritual — particularly the offering to the setting sun, the strict nirjala fast, and the female-led structure — appears to be a distinct regional development of Bihar and the eastern Gangetic plain.

"O Surya, rising to face the world, you fill the eyes of all people with your light. Rising from the ocean, you shine resplendent. We look upon you, O god of the golden rays." — Rig Veda, Mandala 1, Hymn 50 (abridged), c. 1500–1200 BCE

The festival is mentioned in the Mahabharata: Draupadi, the Pandavas' wife, is said to have performed the Chhath ritual on the advice of the sage Dhaumya to ensure the Pandavas' victory and recovery of their kingdom. Karna, Surya's son, is also associated with sun worship — his daily ritual of standing waist-deep in the Ganga to offer arghya to the sun is a widely cited precedent for Chhath practice. While these epic references do not prove the festival's antiquity, they demonstrate its mythic anchoring in the foundational texts of Hinduism.

Scholars of folk religion note that Chhath shares significant structural features with the sun-worship festivals of the pre-Aryan Munda and Oraon peoples of the Chotanagpur plateau, suggesting a possible Adivasi substratum later absorbed into the Hindu calendar.

Chhath and Migration: The Yamuna Transformation

Chhath was once a deeply regional festival — for centuries, it was virtually unknown outside Bihar and eastern UP. The large-scale migration of Bihari labourers to Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, and Punjab during the 20th century transformed the festival's geography. What began as Bihari migrants celebrating Chhath in their cramped urban quarters has evolved into a massive public phenomenon.

Delhi's experience is the most dramatic. As of 2023, the Delhi government constructs over 1,000 temporary ghats along the Yamuna River and at artificial water bodies across the city for Chhath. The scale is extraordinary — the Yamuna's banks are transformed into a vast ritual landscape with bamboo platforms, lighting, sanitation facilities, and traffic management. An estimated 4 million devotees gather at Delhi's Yamuna ghats during Chhath — a number larger than the attendance of many of the world's biggest music festivals.

Chhath was once invisible to national media — a regional curiosity. Today, Delhi alone constructs over 1,000 temporary ghats for the festival, and the Delhi government declares a public holiday. At Delhi's Yamuna ghats, over 4 million devotees gather — exceeding the attendance of festivals like Glastonbury (UK, 200,000) or Coachella (US, 250,000) by an order of magnitude. The festival's visibility has transformed the political calculus: both AAP and BJP now compete to demonstrate their Chhath-friendliness, funding ghat construction and water treatment in the weeks before the festival.

The environmental paradox is sharp: devotees stand in the heavily polluted Yamuna — one of the world's most contaminated rivers, with faecal coliform levels thousands of times above safe limits — to perform a ritual of purification. The Delhi government has responded by cleaning specific stretches of the river and providing artificial ponds, but the contradiction between ritual purity and environmental reality remains unresolved.

Diaspora: Chhath in Mauritius and the Caribbean

Chhath's global reach is a direct consequence of the indentured labour system (1834–1917), which transported over 1.5 million Indians — a disproportionate number from Bihar and eastern UP — to British, French, and Dutch colonies. In Mauritius, where Indo-Mauritians form the majority, Chhath has been celebrated without interruption since the 19th century and is a national public holiday. The Mauritius celebration is strikingly authentic: the rituals, thekua recipe, Chhath songs in Bhojpuri, and the four-day structure are preserved with a fidelity that sometimes exceeds the festival's observance in urban India.

In Trinidad, Guyana, Suriname, and Fiji — where Bihari indentured labourers formed a significant proportion of the Indian diaspora — Chhath observance declined during the 20th century due to Christian missionary pressure, urbanisation, and cultural assimilation, but has seen a revival since the 1990s as part of a broader reclamation of Indian heritage.

Fun Facts

The Chhath parvaitin's 36-hour nirjala (waterless) fast is one of the most demanding religious fasts in any tradition — more rigorous than the Ramadan fast (which breaks each sunset), the Yom Kippur fast (25 hours but allows water in some traditions), or the Jain Paryushan fasts. Medical studies conducted during Chhath have documented significant metabolic changes, including ketosis, in parvaitins.

The Chhath ritual of offering arghya to the setting sun (Sandhya Arghya) before the rising sun (Usha Arghya) is unique among world sun-worship traditions, which almost universally focus on sunrise. Scholars have suggested this may reflect an ancient understanding of the sun's dual nature (visible and hidden) or an agricultural logic of thanking the sun for both the day past and the day to come.

Chhath songs are a distinctive oral tradition in Bhojpuri, Maithili, and Magahi — passed down exclusively through women and rarely written down. A 2021 folkloric survey documented over 200 distinct Chhath songs, many of them unknown to men in the same communities. The songs cover a remarkable emotional range: devotion to Surya, the pain of fasting, anticipation of the festival, and — in modern compositions — the sorrow of Chhath celebrated far from home by migrant workers.

Artwork References

Madhubani / Mithila Painting: Surya Dev (20th century) — Various collections, including the Crafts Museum, New Delhi. Madhubani paintings from Bihar frequently depict Surya (the sun god) in a chariot drawn by seven horses, a motif closely associated with Chhath. The Mithila region's painting tradition — practised exclusively by women — is the same cultural milieu that sustains the Chhath oral tradition.

Kalighat Painting: Sun Worship (19th century) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. A Kalighat pat showing a devotee offering arghya to the sun, executed in the characteristic bold-outline, watercolour-wash style of 19th-century Kalighat painters. The painting documents the sun-worship ritual in an urban Bengali context.

Folk Art of Bihar: Chhath Scenes (contemporary) — Bihar Museum, Patna. The museum holds a growing collection of folk paintings, sculptures, and photographs documenting Chhath traditions, commissioned as part of a deliberate institutional effort to archive and elevate Bihari cultural heritage.
