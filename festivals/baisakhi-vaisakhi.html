Baisakhi · Vaisakhi

The 1699 founding of the Khalsa, the Panj Pyare, and the world's largest Vaisakhi parade in Surrey, BC

Religion: Sikh (primary), Hindu (solar New Year), Punjabi cultural Key Regions: Punjab, Haryana, Delhi; diaspora (Canada, UK, USA, Australia) Timing: April 13 or 14 (first day of Vaisakh month)

Current Practices

Baisakhi is Sikhism's most important religious festival, commemorating the founding of the Khalsa — the community of initiated Sikhs — by the tenth and final human Guru, Gobind Singh, in 1699. It is also a harvest festival (marking the wheat harvest in Punjab) and a solar New Year across North India. These three meanings — religious, agricultural, calendrical — coexist within a single day, giving Baisakhi a multi-layered character.

Sikh observance centres on the gurdwara: the continuous reading of the Guru Granth Sahib (Akhand Path), followed by a special kirtan (hymn-singing) and katha (discourse) in the morning. The highlight is the Amrit Sanchar — the initiation ceremony in which Sikhs are initiated into the Khalsa through the administration of amrit (sweetened water stirred with a double-edged sword). The day is marked by Nagar Kirtans (community processions) through city streets, led by the Panj Pyare (Five Beloved Ones) in ceremonial dress, with the Guru Granth Sahib carried on a decorated float, and langar (community kitchen) serving free meals to all.

The agricultural Baisakhi involves the first cutting of the wheat crop, community feasts, and folk performances: bhangra, giddha, and Punjabi folk songs. At the Golden Temple in Amritsar, the day draws an estimated 500,000+ pilgrims — one of the largest single-day gatherings in India.

Historical Origins: The 1699 Founding of the Khalsa

The Baisakhi of 1699 — or 1 Vaisakh 1756 in the Sikh Nanakshahi calendar — is one of the most consequential moments in Sikh history. Guru Gobind Singh, the tenth Guru, had called a gathering at Anandpur Sahib. According to Sikh tradition, the Guru emerged from a tent with a drawn sword and asked for a volunteer willing to give his head for the faith. One by one, five men — from five different castes and regions — came forward. Each time, the Guru took the volunteer into the tent and emerged with a bloodied sword, but the volunteers later reappeared alive, having been initiated.

"The Guru then prepared amrit, stirring it with a khanda while reciting the five sacred compositions. He administered it to the Five Beloved Ones, who drank from the same vessel — a deliberate repudiation of caste." — Sikh oral tradition recorded in the 18th-century Gurbilas texts

The Panj Pyare (Five Beloved Ones) were: Daya Ram (a Khatri from Lahore), Dharam Das (a Jat from Hastinapur), Mohkam Chand (a washerman from Dwarka), Sahib Chand (a barber from Bidar), and Himmat Rai (a water-carrier from Jagannath Puri). The five came from five different castes, including those considered "low" in the Hindu caste hierarchy — a deliberate anti-caste statement embedded in the founding ritual of the Khalsa.

The five original Panj Pyare came from five different castes — Khatri, Jat, washerman, barber, and water-carrier — the latter three being considered "shudra" or "untouchable" under the Brahminical varna system. By having all five drink amrit from the same vessel, Guru Gobind Singh enacted a radical ritual repudiation of caste that remains central to Sikh theology. The Khalsa was explicitly conceived as a casteless brotherhood, and the surname "Singh" (lion) was mandated for all initiated Sikh men and "Kaur" (princess) for all initiated Sikh women — erasing the caste-identifying surnames of Hindu tradition.

The Guru then initiated the Panj Pyare into the Khalsa, gave them the five Ks (Kakars) — Kesh (uncut hair), Kangha (wooden comb), Kara (iron bracelet), Kachera (cotton undergarment), and Kirpan (ceremonial sword) — and then asked them to initiate him, reversing the guru-disciple relationship in a gesture of radical equality. The day also marks the Guru's declaration that after him, the Guru Granth Sahib (the Sikh scripture) would be the eternal Guru — ending the line of human Gurus.

Some historians debate the precise details of the 1699 Baisakhi account, noting that the earliest written records date from several decades after the event and were produced in a context of Sikh persecution and state-building. However, the essential historicity of the Khalsa founding at Anandpur in 1699 is not seriously questioned by academic historians, and the event's theological and political significance within Sikhism is independent of variations in the narrative detail.

The Surrey Nagar Kirtan: 500,000 on the Streets of BC

The Baisakhi Nagar Kirtan in Surrey, British Columbia, is the largest Vaisakhi parade outside India and one of the largest annual gatherings in Canada — a country not known for mass street festivals. With an estimated 500,000 attendees, the Surrey parade exceeds the Calgary Stampede (approximately 125,000 daily attendance) and rivals the largest Pride parades and cultural festivals in North America.

The Surrey Vaisakhi parade draws more attendees than the Calgary Stampede. On one day in April, the streets of Surrey — a suburban city in Greater Vancouver — are transformed into a sea of saffron and blue, with floats, kirtan, martial arts displays (gatka), and an extraordinary food operation: langar served on the streets feeds over 100,000 people in one day, entirely free. Food stalls line the parade route for kilometres, offering everything from pakoras and samosas to pizza and ice cream — all donated by Sikh businesses and families.

The Surrey parade's growth — from a few thousand attendees in the 1990s to half a million today — maps directly onto the demographic transformation of British Columbia's Lower Mainland. Sikhs now form approximately 13% of Surrey's population, concentrated in neighbourhoods with gurdwaras that function as community, cultural, and political hubs. The Vaisakhi parade has become a civic event as much as a religious one, attended by politicians of all parties, and covered by mainstream Canadian media as a marker of multicultural Canada.

The langar economy: Sikhism's langar tradition — the free community kitchen — achieves its most visible diaspora expression at Vaisakhi parades. The Surrey parade's langar operation involves thousands of volunteers, tonnes of food, and a logistical complexity comparable to a disaster relief operation — but deployed for celebration, not crisis. The economic value of the donated food and labour is estimated in the millions of dollars, all provided as seva (selfless service).

Baisakhi as Harvest Festival and Solar New Year

Before and alongside its Sikh significance, Baisakhi is a harvest festival marking the wheat harvest in Punjab — the region's most important agricultural event. For Punjabi farmers, Baisakhi is a moment of relief and celebration after months of intense labour: the winter wheat crop is ready, the weather is mild (before the punishing Punjab summer sets in), and the agricultural calendar enters a relative pause.

The festival is also celebrated as a solar New Year across northern India: in Bengal (Pohela Boishakh), Assam (Rongali Bihu), Tamil Nadu (Puthandu), Kerala (Vishu), Odisha (Pana Sankranti), and Bihar/Jharkhand (Jur Sital). These New Year celebrations are independent of Baisakhi's Sikh content but share the solar calendar date and root the spring harvest moment in a broader pan-Indian cultural pattern.

The Punjabi cultural Baisakhi involves distinctive folk performances: men performing bhangra (the high-energy folk dance that has become a global phenomenon), women performing giddha (a graceful ring dance accompanied by boliyan — improvised couplets), and the singing of mahiya and tappe (folk songs). The mela (fair) tradition — with swings, food stalls, wrestling bouts, and cultural performances — transforms every Punjabi village and town into a festival ground.

Fun Facts

The name "Baisakhi" derives from the Sanskrit Vaisakha, the second month of the Hindu lunar calendar. In Punjabi phonology, 'v' often becomes 'b' — hence Baisakhi. The same phonological shift gives us "Banaras" from "Varanasi," "Bidya" from "Vidya," and "Bikram" from "Vikram."

The Jallianwala Bagh massacre of April 13, 1919, occurred on Baisakhi day — a fact that adds a profound historical weight to the festival. Thousands had gathered at Jallianwala Bagh in Amritsar to celebrate Baisakhi and peacefully protest the Rowlatt Act when British troops under General Dyer opened fire, killing an estimated 379–1,500 people. The massacre was a watershed moment in the Indian independence movement and is commemorated at the Jallianwala Bagh memorial adjacent to the Golden Temple.

The institution of the langar (free community kitchen) predates the Khalsa by over a century — it was established by Guru Nanak (1469–1539), the first Sikh Guru, as a practice of sitting together and eating together regardless of caste or religion. Guru Amar Das, the third Guru, institutionalised langar by requiring all visitors — including the Mughal emperor Akbar — to eat in the langar before meeting him.

Artwork References

Sikh School Painting: The 1699 Anandpur Baisakhi (19th century) — Government Museum and Art Gallery, Chandigarh. A large-format painting from the Sikh court school showing Guru Gobind Singh administering amrit to the Panj Pyare at Anandpur Sahib, with the dramatic use of colour and composition characteristic of late Sikh painting.

Sikh Arms and Armour Collection — British Museum, London. The museum holds an important collection of Sikh weaponry, including ceremonial swords (kirpans), shields, and armour from the period of the Sikh Empire (1799–1849). These items are directly connected to the Khalsa's martial tradition initiated on Baisakhi 1699.

Ranjit Singh Darbar Paintings (19th century) — Victoria and Albert Museum, London. Paintings of Maharaja Ranjit Singh's court, including depictions of Baisakhi celebrations at the Lahore Darbar, where the festival was observed with military parades, displays of Sikh martial prowess, and distribution of alms.
