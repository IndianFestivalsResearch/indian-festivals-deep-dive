About This Project

Land Of Festivals is a research deep dive into Indian festivals — their regional lives, historical origins, current practices, and diaspora celebrations worldwide. It is designed to be culturally respectful, deeply informative, and fully sourced.

The project draws on public domain primary sources (religious texts, colonial ethnographies, traveler accounts), academic scholarship, and community-based documentation. It covers Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Jain, Buddhist, Christian, Zoroastrian, and Adivasi (Indigenous) traditions.

Guiding Principles

  • See the variation. India is not one festival culture. Every generalisation is qualified.
  • Honour the dispute. When communities disagree about origins or practices, we document the disagreement — not resolve it.
  • Trust lived experience as evidence. Oral testimony is treated with the same methodological care as texts.
  • Anti-exoticise. No "mystical India," "colourful traditions," or tourist-brochure language.
  • Precision over prettiness. "In at least three districts of western Maharashtra, we found evidence that..." not "Maharashtrians believe..."
  • What is not known is as important as what is known. Every festival profile contains an uncertainty section.
  • The diaspora is not a single story. A third-generation Gujarati teenager in New Jersey and a Bhojpuri sugar-cane worker's great-granddaughter in Trinidad have fundamentally different relationships to Indian festivals.
  • Festivals are alive. They change every year. This site is a snapshot, not an eternal description.

Source Policy

All primary source quotations on this site come from public domain texts (published before 1928). These include:

  • Sacred texts in 19th-century translations (sacred-texts.com, archive.org)
  • Colonial ethnographies (W. Crooke, 1896; Imperial Gazetteer of India, 1908-09)
  • Traveler accounts (Ibn Battuta 14th c.; Nicolo Conti 15th c.; Domingo Paes 16th c.)
  • Mughal court chronicles (Ain-i-Akbari, Jahangirnama)

Modern academic sources are cited under fair use/fair dealing for short quotations. Full bibliographic details are available in the master source index.

Ethics & Positionality

This project follows strict ethical guidelines:

  • No flattening India into one uniform tradition
  • No overemphasising North Indian or English-language perspectives
  • No ignoring minority, rural, or marginalised communities
  • No confusing mythological meaning with historical evidence
  • Restricted ritual spaces and sacred knowledge are respected — not infiltrated
  • Interview subjects are anonymised by default with informed consent
  • All researchers disclose their positionality (insider/outsider status)

Research Blueprint

This website is built from a comprehensive research blueprint covering:

  • 20+ festivals across eight religious and community traditions
  • 28+ Indian states with regional variation mapping
  • Six historical layers from ancient textual evidence to the digital era
  • Six diaspora types from indenture labourers to skilled professionals
  • 74 verified public domain primary source URLs

The full blueprint and data files are available in the festival_data/ directory of this project.
